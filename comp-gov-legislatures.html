Legislatures

Legislatures may play a fundamental role in ensuring popular, representative and accountable government. Yet they vary in composition, structure (bicameralism or unicameralism) and size, and have different relationships with executives and bureaucracies. They also display diverse ways of organising their business internally through political parties, committees and speakers, which shape their decision-making. Does this institutional diversity impact on the character and quality of democracy? Central questions within this topic fall into three categories: What shapes the role that legislatures play in democratic governance? How does the structure and composition of legislatures affect the character and quality of democratic governance? How can we evaluate the process and results of legislative decision-making? Two approaches to the study of legislatures dominate the literature. One is inductive, seeking to develop knowledge from careful study of individual cases. The other is deductive, drawing upon social choice theory and formal institutional modelling.

Essay Questions

  1. What explains variation in the institutional design of modern legislatures?
  2. Describe the differences in the legislature’s role in presidential and parliamentary systems and analyse the consequences for government policy.

Textbook Level Introductions

  • Laver, M. (2006) Legislatures and Parliaments in Comparative Context, in Weingast, B. and Wittman, D. eds. Oxford Handbook of Political Economy (Oxford: Oxford University Press) Ch. 7.
  • Carey, J.M. (2008) Legislative Organizations in The Oxford Handbook of Political Institutions (Oxford: Oxford University Press) Ch. 22.
  • Strøm, K., Müller, W. and Bergman, T. (2003) Delegation and Accountability in Parliamentary Democracies (Oxford: Oxford University Press). Particularly Ch. 3 on the main differences in the relationship of the legislature to the executive.
  • Tsebelis, G. (2002) Veto Players: How Political Institutions Work (Princeton: Princeton University Press) Chs. 4 and 7. Theoretical approach to legislative-executive relations that reconceptualizes legislative power by reference to agenda-setting capacities of governments, and the number of veto players in the assembly.

Further Introductory Readings

  • Loewenberg, G., Squire, P. and Kiewiet, D.R. (2002) Legislatures: Comparative Perspectives on Representative Assemblies (Ann Arbor: University of Michigan Press).
  • Olson, D. (1994) Democratic Legislative Institutions: A Comparative View (Armonk, NY: M.E. Sharpe) esp. Chs. 3–5. A basic overview of key institutional features of legislatures.
  • Uslaner, E.M. and Zittel, T. (2011) Comparative Legislative Behavior, in Rhodes, R., Binder, S. and Rockman, B. eds. The Oxford Handbook of Political Institutions (Oxford: Oxford University Press).

On Legislative Institutionalization

  • Carroll, R., Cox, G.W. and Pachon, M. (2006) How Parties Create Electoral Democracy, Part 2, Legislative Studies Quarterly 31(2): 153–174.
  • Andrews, J.T. (2017) Institutionalising Parliaments in Central and Eastern Europe, in Fagan, A. and Kopecký, P. eds. The Routledge Handbook of East European Politics (Routledge) pp. 82–99.
  • Congleton, R.D. (2011) Perfecting Parliament: Constitutional Reform, Liberalism, and the Rise of Western Democracy (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press).
  • Cox, G.W. (2006) The Organization of Democratic Legislatures, in Weingast, B.R. and Wittman, D.A. eds. The Oxford Handbook of Political Economy (Oxford: Oxford University Press).
  • Cox, G.W. and McCubbins, M.D. (2011) Managing Plenary Time: The US Congress in Comparative Context, in Lee, F.E. and Schickler, E. eds. The Oxford Handbook of the American Congress (Oxford: Oxford University Press).
  • Gallagher, M., Laver, M. and Mair, P. (2011) Representative Government in Modern Europe: Institutions, Parties, and Governments (Boston: McGraw Hill) Ch. 3.
  • Krehbiel, K. (1991) Information and Legislative Organization (Ann Arbor: Michigan University Press) Chs. 1 and 7.
  • Rasch, B. (2014) Institutional Foundations of Legislative Agenda-Setting, in Martin, S., Saalfeld, T. and Strøm, K. eds. The Oxford Handbook of Legislative Studies (Oxford: Oxford University Press) pp. 456–473.
  • Zubek, R. (2011) Negative Agenda Control and Executive-Legislative Relations in Central Europe, Journal of Legislative Studies 17(2).

On Legislative Voting

  • Bowler, S., Farrell, D.M. and Katz, R.S. (1999) Party Discipline and Parliamentary Government (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) Chs. 1–2.
  • Carey, J.M. (2007) Competing Principals, Political Institutions, and Party Unity in Legislative Voting, American Journal of Political Science 51(1): 92–107.
  • Carey, J.M. (2009) Legislative Voting and Accountability (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) Chs. 1, 5 and 6.
  • Andeweg, R.B. and Thomassen, J. (2011) Pathways to Party Unity: Sanctions, Loyalty, Homogeneity and Division of Labour in the Dutch Parliament, Party Politics 17(5): 655–672.
  • Cox, G.W. and McCubbins, M.D. (2007) The Legislative Leviathan (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) Ch. 6.
  • Cox, G.W., Heller, W.B. and McCubbins, M.D. (2008) Agenda Power in the Italian Chamber of Deputies, 1988–2000, Legislative Studies Quarterly 33(2): 171–198.
  • Diermeier, D. and Feddersen, T. (1998) Cohesion in Legislatures and the Vote of Confidence Procedure, American Political Science Review 92: 611–621.
  • Kam, C. (2009) Party Discipline and Parliamentary Politics (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) Chs. 1, 2 and 10.
  • Sieberer, U. (2006) Party Unity in Parliamentary Democracies: A Comparative Analysis, Journal of Legislative Studies 12(2): 150–178.
  • Tavits, M. (2009) The Making of Mavericks: Local Loyalties and Party Defection, Comparative Political Studies 42(6): 793–815.

Categorizing Legislatures and Assessing their Power and Effectiveness

  • Arter, D. (2007) Comparing and Classifying Legislatures (London: Routledge). Also published as special issues of the Journal of Legislative Studies 2006, 12(3–4).
  • Polsby, N. (1990) Legislatures, in Norton, P. ed. Legislatures (Oxford: Oxford University Press) 129–148. Introduces the classic differentiation between arena and transformative assemblies.
  • Primo, D.M. (2007) Rules and Restraint: Government Spending and the Design of Institutions (Chicago: Chicago University Press). An important study showing the effect of legislative design upon the formation of fiscal policy.
  • Cox, G. and Morgenstern, S. (2001) Latin America’s Reactive Assemblies and Proactive Presidents, Comparative Politics 33(2): 171–189.
  • Cameron, C.M. (2000) Veto Bargaining (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press). On presidential interaction with the assembly and the impact that presidential vetoes have on legislative decision-making.
  • Morgenstern, S. and Nacif, B. (2002) Legislative Politics in Latin America (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press). Especially Chs. 6, 8 and 11.

The Structure and Composition of Legislatures

  • Shepsle, K.A. and Bonchek, M.S. (1997) Analyzing Politics: Rationality, Behavior, and Institutions (New York: W.W. Norton) Chs. 12, 14 and Ch. 5 from p. 115. A good introduction to rational choice theory as applied to legislative decision-making and its consequences for understanding the role of parties, committees, and procedures.
  • Cox, G. (2006) The Organization of Democratic Legislatures, in Oxford Handbook of Political Economy (Oxford: Oxford University Press). For wide-ranging and very detailed empirical information.
  • Yordanova, N. (2009) The Rationale behind Committee Assignment in the European Parliament: Distributive, Informational and Partisan Perspectives, European Union Politics 10(2): 253–280.

On Parliamentary and Congressional Parties

  • Cox, G. (1987) The Efficient Secret: The Cabinet and the Development of Political Parties in Victorian England (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press). An exemplary case study of how and why parliamentary organisation changed over time in the UK.
  • Cox, G. and McCubbins, M. (2007) Legislative Leviathan: Party Government in the House, 2nd ed. (Berkeley: University of California Press) Chs. 4–5. Argues that parties are crucial to structuring the legislative process in the US Congress.
  • Bowler, S. (2002) Parties in Legislatures: Two Competing Explanations, in Dalton, R.J. and Wattenberg, M.P. eds. Parties without Partisans: Political Change in Advanced Industrial Democracies (Oxford: Oxford University Press) 157–179. Compares two conceptions of the role of parties in legislatures.
  • Müller, W. (2000) Political Parties in Parliamentary Democracies: Making Delegation and Accountability Work, European Journal of Political Research 37(3): 309–333. Uses a principal-agent approach to argue that strong parties are crucial to a legislature’s healthy operation.

On the Effects of Legislative Institution Design

  • Döring, H. (2001) Parliamentary Agenda Control and Legislative Outcomes in West Europe, Legislative Studies Quarterly 26(1): 145–165. A comprehensive analysis of the impact of agenda-setting rules on legislative outcomes in Western Europe.
  • Huber, J.D. (1992) Restrictive Legislative Procedures in France and the United States, American Political Science Review 86: 675–687.
  • Saalfeld, T. (2000) Members of Parliament and Governments in Western Europe: Agency Relations and Problems of Oversight, European Journal of Political Research 37(3): 353–376. A wide-ranging analysis of the means by which legislatures can hold executives to account.
  • Franchino, F. and Hoyland, B. (2009) Legislative Involvement in Parliamentary Systems: Opportunities, Conflict, and Institutional Constraints, American Political Science Review 103(4): 607–621.

On Second Chambers

  • Roberts Clark, W., Golder, M. and Golder, S. (2013) Principles of Comparative Politics (Washington DC: CQ Press) Ch. 14.
  • Heller, W.B. (2007) Divided Politics: Bicameralism, Parties, and Policy in Democratic Legislatures, Annual Review of Political Science 10: 245–269. An excellent review of the literature.
  • Cutrone, M. and McCarty, N. (2006) Does Bicameralism Matter?, in Oxford Handbook of Political Economy (Oxford: Oxford University Press) Ch. 10.
