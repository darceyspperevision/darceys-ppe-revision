Hills (2011)

Hills, J. (2011) “The changing architecture of the UK welfare state”, Oxford Review of Economic Policy, 27 (4): 589–607.

Overview

An academic article by John Hills tracing the shifting balance between public and private welfare in the UK from 1979 to 2010, and projecting the impact of Coalition austerity. It constructs an eight-category matrix measuring welfare activity across public/private provision, finance, and control, tracking changes across the Conservative era (1979–95), New Labour (1995–2007), and austerity (2007 onwards). Key finding: total welfare activity grew by over 150% in real terms, but the “pure public” share only fell from 52% to 48%. Housing was the major outlier, with pure private activity dominating. Coalition cuts, Hills projected, would restructure the state toward the elderly and residualise working-age benefits — pushing the UK toward a stigmatised, US-style system. Also incorporates Titmuss’s fiscal welfare concept.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Total welfare activity expanded enormously despite attempts to cap public spending.
Total welfare activity grew by over 150% in real terms between 1979–80 and 2007–08, increasing from 34% to 42% of GDP.
The fundamental architecture of the welfare state is highly resistant to radical transformation.
The pure public share only dropped from 52% (1979) to 48% (2007), and the proportion under public control barely declined at all (remaining near 61%).
Housing is the major anomaly where state withdrawal was severe.
Pure private housing activity grew from £61bn (1995–96) to £103bn (2007–08), dominating the sector at three-quarters of total housing activity.
Coalition austerity will restructure the welfare state toward the elderly, exacerbating inequality.
Health and pensions projected to consume 34.8% of public services spending by 2014–15, while working-age benefits faced real-term cuts of 10–21%.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Mixed Economy of Welfare
The structural balance between public and private provision, finance, and control within welfare services.
Fiscal Welfare State
Government welfare support delivered indirectly through tax concessions and reliefs — Titmuss’s concept.
Vertical Equity
Providing more favourable treatment to those with lower incomes to reduce inequality.
Horizontal Equity
Providing compensatory treatment to those with similar incomes but greater needs (e.g. disability, larger families).
Life-cycle Redistribution
Providing cash and in-kind services during low-income life stages (childhood, old age) offset by contributions during higher-earning periods.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency vs. equality is a complex oscillation: Thatcher abandoned US-style private health insurance specifically because of severe efficiency costs and market failures — efficiency concerns can actually preserve public provision.
  • Equality requires public finance, but this clashes with efficiency disincentives: Blair’s shift toward private pensions and means-testing was reversed because it created complexity and savings disincentives — an efficiency failure caused by equality-targeting.
  • Default marketisation harmed equality: Because public services couldn’t keep up with demand, pure private grew by default. Since private finance is not redistributive, welfare growth disproportionately benefited those who could pay.
  • Coalition austerity = simple exacerbation of inequalities if working-age cuts proceed without protecting lower-income recipients — demonstrating equality is subordinated to fiscal efficiency.
