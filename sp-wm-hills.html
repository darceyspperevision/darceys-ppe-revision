Hills (2011)

Hills, J. (2011) “The changing architecture of the UK welfare state”, Oxford Review of Economic Policy, 27 (4): 589–607.

What This Source Is

An academic article by John Hills examining how the boundaries between the state and the private sector have evolved across five core areas of welfare activity (education, health care, income maintenance, housing, and personal care) in the UK from 1979 through the New Labour years up to 2010. It also analyses the anticipated impacts of the 2010 Conservative-Liberal Coalition government’s spending cuts and reforms.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This article aligns perfectly with Topic 3 as it directly analyses the shifting dynamics of the “mixed economy of welfare” over thirty years. It breaks down the welfare mix into three distinct dimensions: provision (public vs. private providers), finance (tax-funded vs. private payments or tax reliefs), and decision/control (state allocation vs. consumer choice). Furthermore, it addresses the “social divisions of welfare” by explicitly incorporating Richard Titmuss’s concept of the “fiscal welfare state” (tax concessions) and occupational pensions into its data. Finally, it directly engages with the implications of privatisation, noting that private financing lacks the redistributive qualities of public welfare, which inherently affects equality and welfare outcomes.

Overview

The article traces architectural shifts in the UK welfare state by constructing an eight-category matrix that measures welfare activity based on combinations of public and private provision, finance, and control.

  • The Conservative Era (1979–1995): The government attempted to “roll back the state” and keep public spending constrained. However, total welfare activity grew rapidly. The most decisive shift towards privatisation occurred in housing, through policies like the Right to Buy and the transfer of stock to housing associations, which massively expanded the “pure private” sector.
  • The New Labour Era (1995–2007): Public spending increased significantly, particularly in health and education, but the government avoided raising the overall tax burden drastically. Despite political ambitions, the overarching architecture of the welfare mix changed at a “glacial” pace. The most notable trend was the continued, default growth of the “pure private” sector, as rising affluence and an ageing society drove demand for health, education, and pensions that state provision could not fully satisfy.
  • Recession and Coalition Austerity (2007–2010 and beyond): The financial crisis caused social spending to spike to over 30% of GDP due to falling economic output and stimulus efforts. The 2010 Coalition government responded with selective, severe spending cuts. Hills projects that these cuts will fundamentally restructure the welfare state, protecting services for the elderly (health and pensions) while heavily residualising benefits and services for the working-age population, pushing the UK toward a more stigmatised, US-style system.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Total welfare activity has expanded massively, regardless of government attempts to cap public spending. As societies age and grow more affluent, demand for health, education, and pensions inevitably rises.
Total welfare activity (public and private combined) grew by over 150% in real terms between 1979–80 and 2007–08, increasing from 34% to 42% of GDP.
The fundamental “architecture” of the welfare state is highly resistant to radical transformation. Despite the political rhetoric of Thatcher and Blair, the balance of the mixed economy has changed very slowly.
The share of the “pure public” sector only dropped from 52% in 1979 to 48% in 2007, and the proportion of services under public control barely declined at all (remaining near 61%).
Housing is an anomaly where state withdrawal has been severe. Housing has always held an ambiguous place in the welfare state, and it experienced outright privatisation.
“Pure private” housing activity grew from £61 billion in 1995–96 to £103 billion in 2007–08, completely dominating the sector to represent three-quarters of total housing activity.
The Coalition’s austerity measures will restructure the welfare state towards the elderly, exacerbating inequality. The selective nature of the planned cuts alters the demographic balance of the state.
According to the 2010 Spending Review, health and pensions are projected to consume 34.8% of public services spending by 2014–15, while working-age transfers, education, and other public services face real-term cuts of 10% to 21% relative to their 2010 levels.

Key Terms & Concepts

Mixed Economy of Welfare
The structural balance between public and private provision, finance, and control within welfare services.
Pure Public Sector
Welfare services that are entirely publicly provided, publicly financed, and publicly decided upon (e.g. state schools).
Pure Private Sector
Welfare services that are privately provided, privately financed, and privately decided upon (e.g. private renting without housing benefit).
Fiscal Welfare State
Government welfare support provided indirectly through tax concessions and reliefs — a concept originally coined by Richard Titmuss.
Vertical Equity
The principle of providing more favourable treatment to those with lower incomes to reduce the depth of poverty and overall inequality.
Horizontal Equity
Providing compensatory treatment to individuals who have similar incomes but greater needs, such as those with disabilities or larger families.
Life-cycle Redistribution
The provision of cash and in-kind services during stages of life when market income is low (childhood and old age), which is offset by contributions made during higher-earning periods.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Based strictly on the source, the changes in the UK welfare mix are constrained and driven by a complex clash between both efficiency and equality, rather than one universally dominating the other.
  • Efficiency market failures justify strong state retention: Attempts to radically privatise often fail due to efficiency costs. For example, Margaret Thatcher’s governments abandoned the idea of shifting to a US-style private health insurance system specifically because of the severe efficiency costs and private insurance market failures involved.
  • Equality requires public finance, but this clashes with efficiency disincentives: While private markets can provide services efficiently, they leave poverty and inequality at socially undesirable levels, requiring the state to intervene for vertical and horizontal equity. However, when governments try to save money via means-testing, it harms efficiency. Tony Blair’s government attempted to shift pension funding toward the private sector and increase means-testing, but this was reversed because it created massive complexity and savings disincentives — an efficiency failure.
  • Recent reforms risk disastrous efficiency side-effects in the pursuit of equality: The Coalition government’s policy of shifting university funding to private finance while offering means-tested bursaries creates overlapping means-tests. The author warns that stacking these systems creates effective marginal tax rates approaching 100%, which is a massive efficiency and labour market disincentive.
  • Default marketisation has harmed equality: Because public services have not grown fast enough to meet demand, the “pure private” sector has expanded by default. Because private finance is generally not redistributive, the growth in welfare activity has disproportionately benefited those who can pay, harming equality. Furthermore, if the Coalition proceeds with cuts to working-age benefits without protecting lower-income recipients, “the result is a simple exacerbation of existing inequalities.”
  • In summary, the text demonstrates that policymakers constantly oscillate between these two concerns: radical privatisation is often blocked by the inefficiency of private market failures, while attempts to maintain equality on a budget repeatedly fail due to the inefficient behavioural disincentives they create.
