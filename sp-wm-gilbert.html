Gilbert (2015)

Gilbert, N. (2015) “Restructuring the mixed economy of welfare: three modes of privatization”, European Policy Analysis, 1(1): 41–55.

What This Source Is

An academic article examining how advanced industrialized nations within the OECD are reforming their social protection systems. Specifically, the article explores the transition away from publicly delivered and financed welfare toward a mixed economy characterized by increased private sector involvement, categorizing this privatization into three distinct modes: direct spending, indirect spending, and regulatory methods.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

Topic 3 focuses on “The Changing Nature of the Welfare Mix,” including the shifting roles of the state, market, and voluntary sectors, why these changes occurred, and the implications of privatizing finance, provision, and regulation. This source is highly relevant because it provides a comprehensive framework for understanding how and why the welfare mix has shifted. It details the transition from the traditional “Welfare State” to the “Enabling State,” mapping out exactly how the responsibilities of public and private agents are being redefined. Crucially, it directly addresses the prompt’s question about the privatization of finance, provision, and regulation by outlining three specific modes of privatization (Direct, Indirect, and Regulatory) and evaluating their varying effects on welfare outcomes and market efficiency.

Overview

The article begins by outlining the massive structural pressures forcing OECD countries to restructure their welfare states. Demographic shifts — such as rapid population aging, the rise of one-person households, and an increase in single-parent families — have placed tremendous fiscal strain on the younger working generation. Concurrently, economic globalization has increased capital mobility, forcing nations to scale back labor rights and welfare benefits to lower production costs and remain competitive. Additionally, policy makers have increasingly recognized that generous, passive welfare benefits can inadvertently create “dependency” or “poverty traps,” prompting a shift in ideology.

In response to these pressures, welfare states have altered their fundamental policies, shifting from a traditional “Welfare State” to an “Enabling State.” This shift is characterized by moving away from universal entitlement and public provision toward selective targeting, work-oriented incentives, and privatization.

The core of the paper identifies three modes of privatization:

  • Direct Spending (Vouchers and Third-Party Contracts): The government directly uses tax revenue to pay private (for-profit or voluntary) entities to deliver services. This includes vouchers (like housing or school vouchers) and outsourcing services via third-party contracts. However, Gilbert notes that these contracts often fail to generate true market efficiency because the government buys the service but doesn’t consume it, while the citizen consumes it but doesn’t pay. Thus, cost savings often come from utilizing cheaper, non-unionized private labor rather than genuine market innovation.
  • Indirect Spending (Tax Expenditures and Public/Private Partnerships): This involves partial and compensatory government subsidies for privately financed welfare. Examples include tax deductions for private health insurance or pensions (tax expenditures), and Social Impact Bonds where private investors fund a social program and are only reimbursed by the government if measurable outcomes are achieved, shifting the financial risk away from the state.
  • Regulatory Methods: The government uses legislative mandates to force the private market to assume welfare costs, such as mandating employers to provide sick pay or private old-age benefits. This allows governments to expand welfare without raising taxes.

Gilbert concludes that because tax revenues in OECD countries have largely plateaued, governments are reaching a “tax ceiling.” Consequently, future privatization will likely rely heavily on indirect subsidies and regulatory mandates rather than direct spending, positioning the welfare state as a “handmaiden” to the market economy.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The traditional welfare state is being fundamentally restructured into an “enabling state” focused on targeted benefits and work activation.
The author points to specific global policy reforms, such as the Hartz Reforms in Germany, the Revenu de Solidarite Active in France, and Universal Credit in the United Kingdom, all designed to move beneficiaries into the paid labor force.
Privatizing the delivery of social services via third-party contracts does not inherently create market discipline or efficiency.
Gilbert argues that the “third-party” dynamic breaks the consumer-buyer feedback loop. He provides evidence that cost savings in the US are often merely the result of employing non-unionized workers (private sector unionization was 7.2% compared to 37% in the public sector), rather than superior operational efficiency.
Fiscal constraints will force governments to rely on indirect and regulatory modes of privatization in the future.
OECD data shows that average tax rates plateaued and slightly declined from 35.2% to 33.8% between 2000 and 2010, limiting direct spending. Meanwhile, private pension plans have exploded, with participation jumping from 77 to 125 million people (1990–2008), heavily subsidized by favorable tax treatments like 401k accounts.

Key Terms & Concepts

The Enabling State
A remodeled welfare state paradigm that shifts focus from protecting labor and universal entitlement to promoting work, social responsibility, and private delivery.
Poverty/Dependency Traps
Unintended consequences of generous social security systems where benefits inhibit an individual’s inclination to seek work.
Schumpeterian Workfare State
A concept contrasting Keynesian state intervention, emphasizing instead the fiscal limits of taxation and the need to prioritize innovative activity and work-oriented welfare reforms.
Third-Party Contracts (Outsourcing)
Arrangements where the government allocates public funds to contract with voluntary or for-profit organizations for the production and delivery of social services.
Proxy-shopping
A proposed mechanism for third-party contracts requiring private providers to attract paying customers, whose behavior acts as an indirect monitor of cost and quality for public funders.
Tax Expenditures
Indirect public spending via tax deductions, exemptions, or credits that subsidize a citizen’s private purchase of social benefits (e.g. daycare or housing).
Public/Private Partnership (PPP) / Social Impact Bonds
Arrangements where private investors provide start-up funds for a human service program and are reimbursed with a profit by the government only if successful outcomes are achieved.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency and Cost Reduction as Primary Drivers: The source strongly indicates that changes in the UK welfare mix have been heavily driven by concerns over efficiency, cost, and fiscal constraints. The author notes that “skyrocketing costs” due to an aging population and the pressures of economic globalization have forced OECD countries, including the UK, to aggressively heighten efficiency.
  • Outsourcing: The UK more than tripled the proportion of public expenditures on personal social services contracted out to the private sector (from 11% to 34%) between 1979 and 1996 to cut operational costs.
  • Shifting Financial Risk: Britain pioneered the “Social Impact Bond” in 2010 to fund prisoner rehabilitation. This was heavily driven by efficiency concerns, as it shifts the large start-up costs and financial risks of social programs away from the public sector and onto private investors.
  • Pensions: The UK is listed among countries actively incorporating private individual accounts into mandatory pension systems to alleviate the massive future fiscal burden of state pensions.
  • Redefining Equality as “Equity” and “Work”: Instead of ignoring equality, the changes reframe it. The traditional egalitarian goal of “universal entitlement” has been replaced by “selective targeting” under the explicit aim of “Restoring social equity.”
  • Workfare over Passive Equality: The UK’s introduction of “Universal Credit” is cited as a prime example of moving away from passive, equal benefit distributions toward “work-oriented measures.” This was driven by the realization that generous welfare benefits were creating “dependency traps.” Therefore, the UK’s changes were driven by a desire to combine market efficiency (activation into the labor market) with a new form of equality that focuses on social inclusion through employment rather than universal cash handouts.
  • Conclusion: Recent changes in the UK welfare mix have been fundamentally catalyzed by urgent efficiency concerns (fiscal pressure, globalization, demographic aging), which subsequently led to the abandonment of universal equality in favor of targeted equity, risk-shifting, and labor-market activation.
