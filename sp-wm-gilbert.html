Gilbert (2015)

Gilbert, N. (2015) “Restructuring the mixed economy of welfare: three modes of privatization”, European Policy Analysis, 1(1): 41–55.

Overview

Gilbert maps the transition of OECD welfare states from the “Welfare State” to the “Enabling State” — a shift from universal entitlement and public provision toward selective targeting, work-oriented incentives, and privatisation. He identifies three modes of privatisation: (1) Direct spending (vouchers, third-party contracts); (2) Indirect spending (tax expenditures, Social Impact Bonds); (3) Regulatory methods (legislative mandates forcing private actors to assume welfare costs). Key critique: third-party contracting does not generate genuine market efficiency — cost savings come from cheaper, non-unionised labour, not innovation. Given OECD tax ceilings, future privatisation will rely heavily on indirect and regulatory modes.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The traditional welfare state is being restructured into an “enabling state.”
Specific reforms cited: Hartz Reforms (Germany), Revenu de Solidarité Active (France), Universal Credit (UK) — all designed to move beneficiaries into paid labour.
Third-party contracts do not inherently create market efficiency.
The third-party dynamic breaks the consumer-buyer feedback loop. US evidence: cost savings often reflect employing non-unionised workers (private sector unionisation 7.2% vs. 37% public sector), not operational efficiency.
Fiscal constraints will force reliance on indirect and regulatory privatisation.
OECD average tax rates plateaued from 35.2% to 33.8% (2000–2010). Private pension participation jumped from 77m to 125m people (1990–2008), heavily subsidised by tax treatment.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Enabling State
Remodelled welfare state that promotes work over entitlement, selective targeting over universalism, and private delivery over direct public provision.
Poverty/Dependency Traps
Unintended consequences of generous social security where benefits reduce the incentive to seek work.
Third-Party Contracts
Government allocates public funds to voluntary or for-profit organisations to produce and deliver social services.
Tax Expenditures
Indirect public spending via tax deductions/credits that subsidise citizens’ private purchase of social benefits (e.g. private daycare, housing).
Social Impact Bonds
Private investors fund a social programme and are reimbursed by the government (with profit) only if measurable outcomes are achieved, shifting financial risk from state to market.
Proxy-Shopping
Proposed mechanism requiring private providers to attract paying customers, whose behaviour acts as indirect quality monitor for public funders.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency as the primary driver — empirically strong: “Skyrocketing costs” from ageing populations and globalisation forced OECD countries including the UK to aggressively pursue efficiency. The UK more than tripled the proportion of personal social services contracted out (11% to 34%, 1979–96) to cut costs.
  • UK pioneered efficiency-driven risk-shifting: Britain introduced the Social Impact Bond in 2010 (prisoner rehabilitation) — explicitly shifting financial risk away from the public sector onto private investors.
  • Equality redefined, not abandoned: Universal entitlement replaced by “selective targeting” justified as “restoring social equity.” Universal Credit reframes equality as social inclusion through employment rather than universal cash transfers.
  • Conclusion: Recent UK changes were fundamentally catalysed by fiscal efficiency concerns, which led to the abandonment of universal equality in favour of targeted equity, risk-shifting, and labour market activation.
