Goodair & Reeves (2022)

Goodair, B. and Reeves, A. (2022) “Outsourcing health-care services to the private sector and treatable mortality rates in England, 2013–20: an observational study of NHS privatisation”, Lancet.

Overview

An empirical observational study evaluating the impacts of the 2012 Health and Social Care Act, which outlawed anti-competitive behaviour and prevented NHS commissioners from legally preferring NHS providers over private ones. Using a novel database of £204.1bn of expenditure data from 645,674 individual payments across 173 Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs) (2013–2020), combined with local treatable mortality rates (deaths avoidable with effective medical care), the study finds a clear and significant positive association between for-profit outsourcing and mortality. A 1% annual increase in for-profit outsourcing corresponded with a 0.38% annual increase in treatable mortality the following year — associated with an estimated 557 additional treatable deaths between 2014 and 2019.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The NHS in England has undergone substantial privatisation since 2013.
For-profit outsourcing consistently increased, reaching over 6% of total CCG spending by 2020 (£323m out of £4,999m in just the first three months of 2020).
Outsourcing healthcare to for-profit providers decreases quality and increases mortality.
Multivariate longitudinal regression with fixed effects: a 1% increase in for-profit outsourcing leads to a 0.38% increase in treatable mortality the following year.
The rise in treatable deaths is directly linked to healthcare provision, not general social determinants.
No significant association with “preventable mortality” (public health interventions). Only outsourcing to health-care businesses — not IT, transport, or administration — correlates with rising mortality.
Marketisation principles fail to improve performance.
Expected benefits of competition and management autonomy did not materialise; treatable mortality stagnated, breaking a 10-year trend of improvement.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Treatable Mortality
Deaths that could be avoided with timely, effective healthcare — used as a proxy for healthcare quality.
For-Profit Outsourcing
Commissioning expenditure received by private profit-maximising companies as a percentage of total CCG expenditure (explicitly excludes non-profit/charity).
Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs)
Regional NHS purchasing bodies created by the 2012 reorganisation, responsible for procuring health services.
Cream-Skimming
Practice by for-profit providers of preferentially selecting the most profitable, easily treatable patients, concentrating complex costly cases in public providers.
Creeping Privatisation
The gradual aggregate shift of public service delivery toward independent private-sector providers over time.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency was explicitly the driver: Marketisation of NHS services is “underpinned by beliefs that openness, competition, and management autonomy can improve efficiency and performance of state-funded services.”
  • Efficiency in practice = cost-cutting, not quality: For-profit providers may cut staff numbers, lower qualification levels, or bypass clinical guidelines to maintain profit margins — undermining quality.
  • Market efficiency creates inequality via cream-skimming: FPOs select easy profitable patients; public providers left with complex cases and no extra funding. Deep inequality within the welfare mix.
  • Efficiency-driven changes produced worse welfare outcomes: Hundreds of additional treatable deaths; stagnation in life expectancy improvements. Changes justified by efficiency actively harmed both efficiency and equality.
