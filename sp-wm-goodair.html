Goodair & Reeves (2022)

Goodair, B. and Reeves, A. (2022) “Outsourcing health-care services to the private sector and treatable mortality rates in England, 2013–20: an observational study of NHS privatisation”, Lancet.

What This Source Is

A peer-reviewed observational public health study authored by Benjamin Goodair and Aaron Reeves and published in The Lancet Public Health in 2022. The study empirically evaluates the controversial impacts of the 2012 Health and Social Care Act. By analysing a massive, novel database of procurement contracts, the authors assess whether the increasing outsourcing of National Health Service (NHS) funding to private, for-profit companies is associated with changes in healthcare quality, specifically measured through treatable mortality rates.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source is highly relevant to Topic 3 as it provides a concrete, empirical examination of how the welfare mix in the UK has shifted from a state-based provision model towards a mixed market involving the private sector. Topic 3 questions whether the privatisation of provision has different effects on welfare outcomes and whether public provision is superior to market-based forms. This study directly answers these questions by demonstrating that introducing for-profit providers into the NHS has negatively impacted welfare outcomes. It highlights how legislative changes, specifically the 2012 Health and Social Care Act, forced a change in the welfare mix by outlawing anti-competitive behaviour and preventing NHS commissioning bodies from legally preferring state-owned NHS providers over private companies. The study serves as a critical piece of evidence in the debate over the marketisation of welfare, concluding that the shift toward private and market-based forms of healthcare provision in England has resulted in a decline in healthcare quality, challenging the assumption that market competition inherently improves public services.

Overview

The study investigates the ongoing debate regarding the privatisation of the NHS in England. Following the 2012 Health and Social Care Act, the NHS faced intensified pressure to outsource services to private for-profit providers. Proponents of this marketisation argued that competition would introduce innovative practices and improve efficiency, while critics warned it would lead to dangerous cost-cutting and a decline in patient care.

To evaluate this, the researchers created a novel database encompassing £204.1 billion of expenditure data from 645,674 individual payments made by 173 Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs) between 2013 and 2020. They combined this financial data with local rates of “treatable mortality” — deaths that could be avoided with effective medical intervention, which serves as a proxy for healthcare quality.

The findings definitively show that creeping privatisation has occurred, with for-profit outsourcing consistently increasing from 2013, reaching over 6% of total CCG spending by 2020. Crucially, the statistical analysis revealed a significant positive association between this outsourcing and mortality. An annual increase of one percentage point in for-profit outsourcing corresponded with an annual increase in treatable mortality of 0.38% (or 0.29 deaths per 100,000 population) in the following year.

The authors calculate that between 2014 and 2019, changes in for-profit outsourcing were associated with an estimated 557 additional treatable deaths across the 173 CCGs studied. To prove that this decline in quality was specifically tied to healthcare privatisation rather than broader societal trends (like austerity), the authors demonstrated that mortality increases were only associated with outsourcing to healthcare companies (not transport or IT), and that outsourcing had no impact on preventable mortality (deaths avoided by broader public health interventions). Ultimately, the study suggests that the further expansion of private sector provision in the NHS would be detrimental to population health.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The NHS in England has undergone a substantial period of privatisation since 2013.
By scraping and parsing 12,709 expenditure files from CCG websites, the authors proved that the proportion of total spending going to for-profit companies steadily increased, rising to £323 million (out of £4,999 million) in just the first three months of 2020.
Outsourcing healthcare to for-profit providers decreases the quality of care and increases mortality.
Using multivariate longitudinal regression models with fixed effects, the study found a robust statistical link: a 1% increase in for-profit outsourcing leads to a 0.38% increase in treatable mortality the following year.
The rise in treatable deaths is directly linked to healthcare provision, not general social determinants of health.
The researchers ran alternative models and found no significant association between outsourcing and “preventable mortality” (outcomes linked to general public health interventions rather than acute medical care). Furthermore, they found that only outsourcing to health-care businesses — not IT, transport, or administration companies — correlated with rising mortality.
Marketisation principles fail to improve state-funded healthcare performance.
The expected benefits of openness and management autonomy did not materialise; instead, the 2013–2020 period saw stagnating treatable mortality rates, breaking a 10-year trend of improvement. The authors suggest this may be due to for-profit providers cutting costs (reducing staff numbers or qualifications) or “cream-skimming” profitable patients.

Key Terms & Concepts

Treatable Mortality
A measure of healthcare quality defined as deaths that can mainly be avoided through timely and effective healthcare interventions, including secondary prevention and treatment.
For-profit Outsourcing
Commissioning expenditure that is received by private, profit-maximising companies as a percentage of total expenditure. This explicitly excludes expenditures going to private non-profit organisations or charities.
Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs)
Regional NHS purchasing bodies created by the 2012 reorganisation, responsible for procuring and contracting health services for their registered patients.
Cream-skimming
A practice where for-profit providers preferentially select the most profitable, easily treatable patients, thereby concentrating the most complex, difficult, and expensive cases in public providers.
Creeping Privatisation
The gradual, aggregate shift of public service delivery toward independent, private-sector providers over time.
Covariate Balancing
An advanced statistical matching method used in the study’s models that weights values to balance the data, accounting for differences in observations based on their level of for-profit outsourcing.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Based on the source, the recent changes in the UK’s welfare mix (specifically the privatisation of the NHS) were absolutely driven by concerns about efficiency, but they ultimately resulted in a severe degradation of equality and care outcomes.
  • Efficiency as the primary driver for policy change: The source notes that the marketisation of healthcare services is deeply “underpinned by the beliefs that openness, competition, and management autonomy can improve the efficiency and performance of state-funded services.”
  • Legislation enforcing market efficiency: The 2012 Health and Social Care Act was designed to force this efficiency by outlawing anti-competitive behaviour and opening the market so that NHS providers could not be legally prioritised over private ones.
  • The reality of “efficiency” is cost-cutting: The authors theorise that the pursuit of efficiency by for-profit providers actually manifests as cost-cutting behaviours. Private providers may cut staff numbers, lower qualification levels, or bypass guidelines for correct medical processes to maintain their profit margins, directly undermining the quality of care.
  • Efficiency leads to inequality via “Cream-skimming”: A major consequence of this market-driven efficiency is “cream-skimming,” where for-profit providers preferentially select easy, profitable patients. This creates deep inequality within the welfare mix, as public providers are left with a concentration of the most complicated cases without any extra staff or funding to compensate.
  • Worse welfare outcomes overall: While the changes were justified by efficiency, they have failed to improve performance. Instead, the focus on competition and easily quantifiable outcomes has come at the expense of patient care, contributing to hundreds of additional treatable deaths and stalling improvements in life expectancy across England.
