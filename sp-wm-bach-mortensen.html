Bach-Mortensen & Montgomery (2019)

Bach-Mortensen, A. and Montgomery, P. (2019) “Does sector matter for the quality of care services? A secondary analysis of social care services regulated by the Care Inspectorate in Scotland”, BMJ Open, 2019: 9.

Overview

A quantitative secondary analysis of 13,310 social care organisations regulated by the Care Inspectorate in Scotland, comparing quality outcomes across public, for-profit, and third-sector providers. Evaluated across four quality domains (care and support, management and leadership, environment, staffing), risk classification, and formal complaints/requirements over three years. Uses generalised ordered logistic regression controlling for staff numbers, organisation age, and client group. Findings are unambiguous: public sector (PSO) and third-sector (TSO) providers consistently and statistically significantly outperform for-profit (FPO) organisations on almost all quality outcomes. FPOs more likely to receive merely “adequate” rather than “high quality” ratings.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Organisational sector significantly dictates care quality and risk levels.
Low risk classification: PSOs 91.1%, TSOs 86%, FPOs 79.9%. FPOs most likely to be medium risk (13.2%) or high risk (6.9%).
For-profit organisations are more likely to deliver “adequate” rather than “high quality” care.
Predicted probability of “high quality” score: FPOs 58.4% vs. PSOs 69.2% and TSOs 66.8%. FPOs concentrate in the “adequate” category — consistent with profit-maximising to minimum acceptable standards.
For-profit providers generate significantly higher rates of user complaints and regulatory intervention.
Predicted probability of user complaints (last 3 years): FPOs 10.84% vs. PSOs 5.54% and TSOs 8.65%. FPOs also most likely to have formal requirements imposed following inspection (18.31%).
Differences between public and third sectors are minor compared to both vs. for-profit.
While PSOs occasionally outperformed TSOs slightly, these differences were “substantially smaller” than the performance gap between pro-social sectors and the profit-driven sector.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Quasi-Markets
Markets where the state finances services but providers from all three sectors compete to deliver them.
For-Profit Sector (FPOs)
Privately owned organisations driven by profit maximisation and sustainment.
Public Sector (PSOs)
Publicly owned organisations motivated by social mission and public sector values.
Third Sector (TSOs)
Formally structured, privately owned, non-profit distributing, self-governing organisations pursuing a social mission.
Care Inspectorate
Official regulator of care services in Scotland, responsible for inspection, quality assessment, and risk classification.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Theory promised both efficiency and equity; evidence delivers neither: Quasi-markets were argued to produce “more efficient and equitable service deliveries” — but the authors state there is “little conclusive evidence to support that proposition.”
  • Profit motive = efficiency over peak quality: FPOs are incentivised to organise resources to meet “adequate standards” rather than striving for maximum quality — market efficiency supersedes high-quality equitable public goods.
  • For-profit provision harms equality of outcomes: Higher complaint rates and more formal requirements against FPOs demonstrate that marketisation — driven by efficiency concerns — produces unequal welfare outcomes for service users.
