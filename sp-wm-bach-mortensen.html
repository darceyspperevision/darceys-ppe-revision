Bach-Mortensen & Montgomery (2019)

Bach-Mortensen, A. and Montgomery, P. (2019) “Does sector matter for the quality of care services? A secondary analysis of social care services regulated by the Care Inspectorate in Scotland”, BMJ Open, 2019: 9.

What This Source Is

A peer-reviewed academic journal article published in BMJ Open in 2019. The paper is a quantitative secondary analysis of publicly available data that assesses and compares quality-related outcomes across public, for-profit, and third-sector (voluntary/non-profit) social care organisations in Scotland.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source fits perfectly into Topic 3 because it directly addresses the core debates surrounding the provision of welfare by different sectors.

  • Evaluating Sector Superiority: Topic 3 asks, “Is public and state-based provision of welfare superior to private and market-based forms of provision?” This study explicitly tests this by comparing the performance and quality of care delivered by the state (public sector) versus the market (for-profit sector) and the voluntary sphere (third sector).
  • Quasi-Markets and Privatisation: The paper explores the concept of “quasi-markets” — a central feature of the changing welfare mix where the state finances services, but providers across all three sectors compete to deliver them. The study assesses the practical implications of this marketisation on welfare outcomes.

Overview

The article investigates whether the organisational sector (public, for-profit, or third sector) impacts the quality of social care services. The authors note that health and social care services are increasingly delivered via quasi-markets, where private and third-sector organisations bid for state-funded contracts. While proponents argue this system increases efficiency and equity, there has been little conclusive empirical evidence to support this, particularly in social care settings.

The authors theorise that different sectors operate under fundamentally different incentives: for-profit organisations (FPOs) aim to maximise profits, while public sector organisations (PSOs) and third sector organisations (TSOs) are driven by pro-social values and achieving social missions.

To test how these theoretical differences translate into practical quality, the authors analysed data from the Care Inspectorate, the independent regulator of care services in Scotland. The dataset included 13,310 social care organisations, such as nursing homes, day care centres, and housing support services. They evaluated these organisations across four quality domains (care and support, management and leadership, environment, and staffing), a risk assessment score, and the prevalence of formal requirements or complaints issued against them over the past three years.

Using generalised ordered logistic regression, the researchers controlled for variables like the number of staff, age of the organisation, and client group. The findings overwhelmingly demonstrated that public and third-sector providers perform consistently and statistically significantly better than for-profit organisations on most quality outcomes. PSOs and TSOs were far more likely to receive “high quality” ratings and “low risk” classifications, while FPOs were significantly more likely to receive merely “adequate” scores, be classified as medium or high risk, and receive user complaints or formal requirements. The authors conclude that regulators and policymakers must carefully consider how the underlying profit motives of different sectors impact the management and quality of commissioned public services.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Organisational sector significantly dictates the quality and risk levels of social care.
Public providers had the highest probability of being categorised as “low risk” (91.1%), followed by third-sector providers (86%), with for-profit providers coming in last (79.9%). Conversely, for-profits were the most likely to be rated as medium risk (13.2%) and high risk (6.9%).
For-profit organisations are more likely to deliver “adequate” rather than “high” quality care, likely due to profit-maximisation incentives.
In the “quality of care” domain, the predicted probability for FPOs to attain a “high-quality” score was only 58.4%, compared to 69.2% for PSOs and 66.8% for TSOs. FPOs were far more likely to sit in the “adequate” category. The authors argue this is because rational, profit-driven entities will coordinate resources just enough to meet acceptable standards without expending extra resources to achieve maximum quality.
For-profit providers generate significantly higher rates of user dissatisfaction and regulatory intervention.
FPOs had a predicted probability of 10.84% for receiving user complaints within the last three years, compared to just 5.54% for PSOs and 8.65% for TSOs. FPOs were also the most likely to have requirements imposed on them following an inspection (18.31%).
Differences between the public and third sectors are minor compared to their differences with the for-profit sector.
While PSOs occasionally outperformed TSOs (such as having a slightly higher probability of high-quality staff and management), these statistical differences were “substantially smaller” than the massive performance gap observed between pro-social sectors (PSOs/TSOs) and the profit-driven sector (FPOs).

Key Terms & Concepts

Quasi-markets
Markets where the provision of a service is undertaken by competitive providers (as in pure markets), but the purchasers of the service are financed by the state rather than their own private resources.
For-profit sector (FPOs)
Organisations that are privately owned and administered according to the pursuit, maximisation, and sustainment of profits.
Public sector (PSOs)
Organisations that are publicly owned and administered, motivated by achieving a social mission or public sector motivation/social value.
Third sector (TSOs)
Organisations that are formally structured, privately owned, non-profit distributing, self-governing, and benefiting from voluntary activities. Their purpose is to achieve a social mission and accountability.
Care Inspectorate
The official regulator of care services in Scotland, responsible for regularly inspecting care facilities, assessing the quality of services across specific domains, and distributing risk scores.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Please note: The provided source is a quantitative analysis of care quality outcomes and does not explicitly analyse the historical or political motives (such as specific concerns over efficiency versus equality) that originally drove changes in the UK welfare mix. However, the text does offer the following relevant insights regarding efficiency and equity within this context.
  • Theoretical Promises of Efficiency and Equity: The authors highlight that the shift toward a mixed welfare economy — specifically the introduction of “quasi-markets” — has been historically argued to lead to “more efficient and equitable service deliveries.” Therefore, the system was theoretically designed to address both concepts, though the text states there is “little conclusive evidence to support that proposition.”
  • The Profit Motive vs. Social Value: By increasing the role of for-profit organisations in the welfare mix, the state introduced providers whose primary motivation is to “maximise and sustain profits” (which aligns heavily with operational efficiency and cost-saving). In contrast, public and third-sector providers are motivated by achieving a “social mission” (which aligns more closely with equity and equality of care).
  • Efficiency Prioritised Over Peak Quality: The findings suggest that marketisation and the involvement of for-profits result in resources being managed differently. FPOs are heavily incentivised to organise their resources so that their services fall within “adequate standards” rather than striving for the absolute highest quality. This suggests that a focus on market efficiency and profit margins in the welfare mix often supersedes the delivery of highly equitable, premium-tier public goods.
