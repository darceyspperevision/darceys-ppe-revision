Reading List

Democratization

Democratization in the broadest sense is the process by which polities or aspects of polities become more democratic. More narrowly, political scientists more commonly use the term to refer to the process of transition from an authoritarian to a democratic regime type. The most recent literature emphasizes that transitions to democracy are never fully completed and that democracy deepens gradually, but also stresses that democratization processes are reversible. The central question is: what are the determinants of transition to democracy? Some theorists focus on structural factors such as the class system, the level of economic development, or the nature of political culture; others emphasize the strategies of elite actors; still others emphasize the interaction of these, or the importance of the international dimension.

Essay Questions

  1. Do elite-driven factors matter most for democratization?
  2. Can international actors affect democratization in a country?

Textbook Level Introduction

  • Grugel, J. & Bishop, M.L. (2013). Democratization: A Critical Introduction. Palgrave Macmillan. Introduction.
  • Roberts Clark, W., Golder, M. and Golder, S. (2013) Principles of Comparative Politics. (Washington DC: CQ Press) Chs 6, 7 and 8.
  • Geddes, B., (2009) ‘What Causes Democratization?’ in Boix, C. and Stokes, S.C. eds. The Oxford Handbook of Comparative Politics (Oxford: Oxford University Press) Ch. 14.

Influential and Problem-Framing Texts

  • Huntington, S.P. (1991) The Third Wave: Democratization in the Late Twentieth Century (Norman: University of Oklahoma Press).
  • Linz, J.J. and Stepan, A. (1996) Problems of Democratic Transition and Consolidation: Southern Europe, South America, and Post-Communist Europe (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins UP).
  • Whitehead, L. (2002) Democratization: Theory and Experience (Oxford: OUP).

Socio-economic Explanations and Transitions to Democracy

  • Lipset, S.M. (1959) Political Man: The Social Bases of Politics (London: Heinemann) Ch. 2. A seminal work that sees democracy as the result of modernisation.
  • Przeworski, A. et al. (2000) Democracy and Development: Political Institutions and Well-being in the World, 1950–1990 (Cambridge: CUP) Esp. Ch. 2.
  • Boix, C. and Stokes, S.C. (July 2003) ‘Endogenous Democratization’, World Politics 55(4): 517–549.
  • Acemoglu D. and Robinson, A. (2006) Economic Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy (Cambridge: CUP).
  • Robinson, J.A. (2006) ‘Economic Development and Democracy’ Annual Review of Political Science 9: 503–527.
  • Ansell, B. and Samuels, D. (2014) ‘Economic Development and Democracy’, Annual Review of Political Science 9: 503–527.
  • Bryn Rosenfeld. 2020. The Autocratic Middle Class: How State Dependency Reduces the Demand for Democracy. Princeton University Press.
  • Capoccia, G. and Ziblatt, D. (2010) ‘The Historical Turn in Democratization Studies: A New Research Agenda for Europe and Beyond’, Comparative Political Studies August/September, 43: 931–968.
  • Slater, D. (2009) ‘Revolutions, Crackdowns, and Quiescence: Communal Elites and Democratic Mobilization in Southeast Asia’, American Journal of Sociology 115(1): 203–254.
  • Tudor, Maya (2013) ‘Explaining democracy’s origins: Lessons from South Asia,’ Comparative Politics 45(3): 253–272.

Democracy from Above: Elites and Elite Strategies

  • Przeworski, A. (1991) Democracy and the Market: Political and Economic Reforms in Eastern Europe and Latin America (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press) esp. Chs. 1 and 2.
  • O’Donnell, G., Schmitter, P.C. and Whitehead, L. (1986) Transitions from Authoritarian Rule: Prospects for Democracy: Vol. 4: Tentative Conclusions about Uncertain Democracies (Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press).
  • Mainwaring, S. (1992) ‘Transitions to Democracy and Democratic Consolidation: Theoretical and Comparative Issues’, in Mainwaring, S., O’Donnell, G. and Valenzuela, J.S. eds. Issues in Democratic Consolidation (Notre Dame: University of Notre Dame Press) 294–341.
  • McFaul, M. (2002) ‘The Fourth Wave of Democracy and Dictatorship: Noncooperative Transitions in the Postcommunist World.’ World Politics 54(2): 212–244.

  • Muller, E.N. and Seligson, M.A. (1994) Civic Culture and Democracy: The Question of Causal Relationships, American Political Science Review 88(3): 635–652.
  • Fish, S. (2002) ‘Islam and Authoritarianism’, World Politics 55(1): 4–37.
  • Donno, D. and Russett, B. (2004) ‘Islam, Authoritarianism, and Female Empowerment: What Are the Linkages?’ World Politics 56(4): 582–607.
  • Lust, E. (2011) ‘Missing the Third Wave: Islam, Institutions, and Democracy in the Middle East’, Studies in Comparative International Development 46: 163–190.
  • Kuran, T. (1991) ‘Now out of Never: The Element of Surprise in the East European Revolution of 1989’, World Politics 44(1): 7–48.
  • Finkel, S.E. and Smith, A.E. (2011) ‘Civic Education, Political Discussion, and the Social Transmission of Democratic Knowledge and Values in a New Democracy: Kenya 2002’, American Journal of Political Science 55(2): 417–435.
  • Bunce, V. and S. Wolchik (2011) Defeating Authoritarian Leaders in Post-Communist Countries. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. Chapters 1, 2, 8.
  • Lussier, D.N. and M.S. Fish (2012) ‘Indonesia: The benefits of civic engagement.’ Journal of Democracy 23(1).

International Factors
