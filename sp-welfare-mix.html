Glossary

Key terms and concepts for Topic 3, alphabetically ordered.

Auto-enrolment
A modern policy mechanism (Pensions Act 2008) that automatically places eligible employees into a workplace pension scheme rather than requiring opt-in. Reflects a shift toward quasi-mandatory occupational welfare, designed to counteract the decline in workplace pension coverage and address excessive poverty in old age.
Big Society
David Cameron's political framework emphasising devolution of public services to the Voluntary and Community Sector and local civil society. Critics argue it signals a shift from the welfare state to a “welfare society,” using volunteers and social enterprises to mask reductions in direct state expenditure.
Corporate Welfare
The array of state benefits, subsidies, tax breaks, and services that directly or indirectly meet the needs of private businesses. Often obscured in official data; competes with social welfare for state resources and raises fundamental questions about who the welfare state actually serves.
Corporate–Social Welfare Continuum
An analytical spectrum (Farnsworth) mapping state services based on whose needs they primarily satisfy — from pure social welfare (e.g. social care for citizens) through social-corporate and corporate-social welfare to pure corporate welfare (e.g. business grants and bank bailouts).
Deinstitutionalisation
The post-WWII historical process of depopulating large psychiatric asylums and replacing them with community-based alternatives. Economic crises in the 1970s often led to rapid budget slashing, with the welfare state increasingly reliant on general social programmes to absorb displaced individuals.
Deservingness
The political and social concept determining which vulnerable groups are perceived as morally worthy of welfare support. Currently favours the elderly over the unemployed (“skivers”), shaping the generosity of both statutory and occupational welfare (Naczyk).
Enabling State
A paradigm (Gilbert) representing the transformation of the traditional welfare state: from protecting labour and universal entitlement toward promoting work, social responsibility, selective targeting, and privatisation of delivery. Characterised by workfare, means-testing, and risk-shifting to the market.
Equity–Efficiency Trade-off
The policy dilemma in welfare resource allocation: maximising overall aggregate outcomes (efficiency) versus ensuring fair distribution that prioritises the worst-off (equity). Cadham & Prosser find UK public preferences lean toward equity even when actual policy changes are driven by efficiency concerns.
Fiscal Welfare
One of Titmuss's three social divisions of welfare: benefits delivered indirectly through the tax system (e.g. tax reliefs on mortgages, private pensions, health insurance). Often called the “upside-down welfare state” because it disproportionately benefits middle- and upper-income earners.
Informal Sector / Informal Welfare
Welfare and care provided by family, friends, and neighbours outside formal institutional structures. Feminist critics argue “community care = care by the family = care by women,” enforcing a sexual division of welfare that increases gender inequality.
Mixed Economy of Welfare (MEW)
The recognition that welfare provision is distributed across four sectors: the state, the market, the voluntary/third sector, and the informal (family) sector. The balance changes continuously over time and across policy areas — housing is now predominantly private while health remains mostly public.
Occupational Welfare
Benefits provided by employers above statutory minimums (e.g. occupational pensions, enhanced redundancy pay, medical insurance). Originally a paternalistic “skill retention tool;” its distribution now tracks political perceptions of “deservingness.” Access is deeply unequal across sectors and firm sizes (Naczyk).
Public–Private Welfare Trade-off
The conventional assumption that public and private welfare exist in a zero-sum competition. Horn & Kohl's historical data challenges this: parallel growth is the norm, and strict trade-offs only occur when governments hold strongly anti-interventionist market ideologies.
Pure Private Sector
Welfare that is privately provided, privately financed, and privately decided (e.g. private medical insurance). Has grown substantially — in housing, pure private activity rose from 58% to 82% between 1979 and 2013 (Burchardt & Obolenskaya).
Pure Public Sector
The archetypal post-war welfare state model: publicly provided, publicly financed through taxation, and subject to public decision-making (e.g. an NHS emergency room). Its proportional share of total welfare activity has slowly declined even as the absolute size of the public sector has grown.
Quasi-Markets
A market structure where the government finances services using state resources but introduces competition by allowing organisations from all three sectors to bid for delivery. Intended to combine public funding with market efficiency; evidence (Bach-Mortensen & Montgomery) shows for-profit providers consistently perform worse on quality metrics.
Social Capital
The networks of cooperation, trust, and community relationships governments promote as tools for regenerating deprived areas. Danson & Whittam critique Big Society reliance on social capital, arguing competitive outsourcing destroys rather than builds local community networks.
Social Division of Welfare (SDW)
Titmuss's foundational framework revealing that welfare is delivered through three parallel systems: statutory (public/visible), fiscal (tax-based/hidden), and occupational (employer-based/hidden). Highlights how the “hidden welfare state” channels immense resources to middle- and upper-income groups.
Supply-Side Policy Feedback
Perara's theory: the existence of a public policy creates a workforce to supply it; that workforce then mobilises politically to protect and expand the policy. Explains why public services for marginalised groups persist — the welfare workforce advocates on their behalf out of economic self-interest.
Three-Dimensional View of Welfare
An analytical framework (Powell; Burchardt & Obolenskaya) arguing welfare cannot be understood by looking only at who provides a service. Requires examining (1) Provision — who owns/delivers; (2) Finance — who pays; and (3) Regulation — who controls. The state can exercise vast power without directly providing a service.
Three Modes of Privatisation
Gilbert's typology: (1) Direct spending — vouchers or third-party contracting; (2) Indirect subsidies — tax expenditures or Social Impact Bonds; (3) Regulatory methods — legislative mandates forcing private actors to assume welfare costs (e.g. employer sick pay).
Treatable Mortality
Deaths avoidable with timely, effective healthcare — used as a proxy for healthcare quality. Goodair & Reeves find a 1% annual increase in NHS for-profit outsourcing corresponds with a 0.38% increase in treatable mortality the following year.
VCS / Third Sector
Voluntary and Community Sector: charities, non-profits, and social enterprises operating independently of the state without distributing profits. Evidence (Bach-Mortensen & Montgomery) suggests third-sector providers outperform for-profit providers on most social care quality metrics.
Welfare Dualisation
A consequence of the shifting welfare mix: the state bifurcates, leading to vastly unequal treatment between “insiders” (stable employees who access generous occupational and fiscal welfare) and “outsiders” (precarious workers reliant on diminishing residual public safety nets).
Welfare Workforce
The large segment of the labour market employed directly by the welfare state (nurses, social workers, teachers, administrators). Their unionisation and political mobilisation are key determinants of whether public welfare services survive retrenchment pressures, particularly for marginalised groups who cannot advocate for themselves (Perara).
Wheels of Welfare
Burchardt & Obolenskaya's conceptual framework for unbundling the welfare state: welfare activity assessed across finance, provision, and decision/regulation in each of five major sectors. Allows precise mapping of where and how privatisation is occurring.

Reading List

Topic 3: The Changing Nature of the Welfare Mix

This session introduces debates on the welfare mix. It discusses ways in which the role of the state vis-à-vis the market and voluntary sectors and the family/informal care has changed over time in the UK in terms of benefits and services; why; and with what implications. We also look briefly at the ‘social divisions of welfare’ – not only public, but also fiscal and occupational.

Sample Questions

  1. Does the privatisation of finance, provision and regulation have different effects on welfare outcomes?
  2. Is public and state-based provision of welfare superior to private and market-based forms of provision?
  3. Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality.
