Cadham & Prosser (2023)

Cadham, C. J. and Prosser, L. A. (2023) “Eliciting Trade-Offs Between Equity and Efficiency: A Methodological Scoping Review”, Value in Health, 26 (6): 948–958.

What This Source Is

A systematic methodological scoping review published in Value in Health in 2023. The paper systematically reviews 77 survey-based studies from 29 countries to investigate how societies quantify the trade-off between “equity” (fairness and prioritizing the worst-off) and “efficiency” (maximizing overall health outcomes cost-effectively) when making decisions about the allocation of healthcare resources.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

Topic 3 focuses on debates surrounding the welfare mix, specifically looking at how the roles of the state, market, and voluntary sectors have changed, and the implications of these changes on welfare outcomes (e.g. efficiency versus equality). This source fits directly into this topic by exploring the theoretical and empirical frameworks used to evaluate welfare outcomes — specifically, the tension between maximizing cost-efficiency and ensuring equitable distribution of resources. Economic evaluations of health interventions typically rely on a utilitarian approach, which aligns with market-based or efficiency-driven forms of provision. However, there is growing interest in incorporating health equity into these evaluations to ensure fairness. By reviewing how the public and policymakers value equality versus efficiency, this source provides the methodological context needed to understand how the outcomes of different welfare provisions (public vs. private) can be weighed and measured.

Overview

The paper aims to evaluate how different studies measure society’s willingness to sacrifice efficiency for improvements in equity. To do this, the authors reviewed 77 relevant papers up to June 2021. The authors identified that conventional health economic methods are utilitarian — favouring interventions that provide the most health benefits for the lowest cost (efficiency). However, modern decision-making increasingly seeks to incorporate equity, such as prioritising the worst-off or specific demographics like children.

To analyse this, the researchers divided the 77 studies into two methodological categories:

  • Social Welfare Function Approaches (27 studies): These studies ask respondents to trade off benefits across different groups to calculate an “inequality aversion parameter” — a mathematical representation of how much a society cares about reducing differences in health or income outcomes. The majority of these studies (20 out of 27) found clear evidence that people are averse to inequality, with respondents often valuing health improvements for the worst-off more highly than general gains.
  • Preference Ranking and Distributional Weighting (49 studies): These studies present respondents with various characteristics (e.g. patient age, disease severity, income) and ask them to rank priorities or choose between interventions. The findings here were more mixed: 19 studies showed a clear preference for equity, 27 were mixed, and 3 favoured strict efficiency.

Ultimately, the scoping review concludes that while societies generally demonstrate a willingness to sacrifice some efficiency to achieve greater equity, the sheer variety of study methods, survey designs, and unrepresentative sample sizes makes it difficult for policymakers to apply a universal standard to economic evaluations.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Society generally places a high value on equity and is willing to sacrifice overall efficiency to achieve a fairer distribution of healthcare.
Across the 77 studies reviewed, 39 provided clear evidence of inequality aversion (a preference for equity), 33 found mixed evidence, and only 4 found no evidence of aversion. In the social welfare function studies specifically, 20 out of 27 studies found a distinct aversion to inequality.
Methodological heterogeneity limits the direct comparability of these studies and their usefulness in actual policy decision-making.
The authors found massive variation in how studies were conducted. Survey methods ranged from discrete choice experiments (23 studies) to benefit trade-offs and “leaky bucket” scenarios. Furthermore, studies varied wildly in sample size (from 10 to 4,118 participants) and demographics, with many relying on non-representative samples like students or health policy experts rather than the general public.
Preferences for equity are highly heterogenous and depend on sociodemographic factors and cultural contexts.
Of the 39 studies that evaluated potential demographic differences, 28 found statistically significant differences in preferences. Variables such as age, gender, education, income, and political ideology heavily influenced results; for example, more conservative, higher-income, and male respondents were often found to be less averse to inequality.

Key Terms & Concepts

Utilitarian Approach
A conventional economic resource allocation framework where interventions that provide the maximum overall health benefits cost-effectively are preferred, regardless of how those benefits are distributed.
Cost-Effectiveness Analysis (CEA)
A type of economic evaluation used to determine the value of a health intervention by comparing its costs to the health benefits it produces.
Distributional / Extended CEA
A modern iteration of CEA that measures the distribution of outcomes across different policy-relevant groups (e.g. socioeconomic quintiles) and applies a weight to determine the net equity impact.
Social Welfare Function
A mathematical approach used to measure preferences by having respondents trade off benefits across different groups to generate an “inequality aversion parameter.”
Inequality Aversion
A quantifiable metric that specifies the extent to which an individual or society cares about reducing differences in the distribution of outcomes.
Preference Ranking and Distributional Weighting
An approach that evaluates patient characteristics (e.g. age, lifestyle, disease severity) and health outcomes to generate rankings or weights that help decision-makers target treatments to specific groups.
Veil of Ignorance
A survey framing technique where respondents are asked to make resource allocation choices without knowing what their own position or status will be in the future distribution of outcomes.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Important Caveat: This specific source is a methodological review of global healthcare resource allocation surveys. It does not analyse the history, politics, or recent structural changes of the UK welfare mix, nor does it explore the motivations (such as privatisation) behind policy shifts. Therefore, it cannot directly answer whether actual changes in the UK’s welfare mix were driven more by efficiency or equality. However, the source does provide data on the underlying public and expert preferences in the UK regarding the equity-efficiency trade-off, which informs the debate.
  • High UK Representation: The UK was the most commonly studied location in this global review, accounting for 24 of the 77 studies (7 social welfare studies and 17 preference ranking studies).
  • Preference for Equity over Efficiency: Based on the studies conducted in the UK, there is significant public and expert concern for equality. Out of the 24 UK studies, 10 showed clear evidence of aversion to inequality (favouring equity), 12 showed mixed results, and only 2 showed a clear preference for efficiency without equity concerns.
  • Conclusion drawn from the source: While the source cannot tell us why the UK government has altered the welfare mix in recent years, it definitively shows that when UK citizens and policymakers are polled on welfare outcomes, there is a distinct and prevailing desire to ensure equality (equity), rather than focusing solely on cost-efficiency.
