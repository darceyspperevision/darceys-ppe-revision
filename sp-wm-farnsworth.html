Farnsworth (2013)

Farnsworth, K. (2013) “Bringing corporate welfare in”, Journal of Social Policy, 42(1): 1–22.

Overview

Farnsworth argues that social policy analysis has a serious omission: the vast amount of public resources directed at private corporations (“corporate welfare”). He extends Titmuss’s three divisions of welfare by adding a fourth — corporate welfare — and creates a corporate–social welfare continuum mapping services from pure social welfare to pure corporate welfare. Key insight: countries with the most generous social welfare systems (Sweden, Denmark) are also among the most generous in corporate welfare, suggesting they are complementary, not competing. Contextualised by the 2008 crisis: massive bank bailouts constituted a colossal expansion of corporate welfare, the costs of which were then shifted onto citizens via social welfare cuts.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Corporate welfare is deeply embedded; “independent” capitalism is a myth.
The state subsidises transport, research, public health (boosting worker productivity), and executed massive post-2008 bailouts of Northern Rock, RBS, and HBOS.
High social welfare does not harm economic competitiveness.
OECD data plotted on a scatterplot shows a positive correlation: Denmark, Sweden, and France have high levels of both social and corporate welfare simultaneously.
The 2008 crisis constituted a massive wealth transfer to corporations at citizens’ expense.
UK governments pumped hundreds of billions into banking systems; subsequent IMF-prescribed austerity cuts shifted accumulated debt costs onto citizens, drastically widening inequality.
Social welfare policies actually serve the needs of private corporations.
Unemployment benefits are counter-cyclical (boosting consumption in downturns) and allow labour market flexibility. State education socialises the costs of workforce training.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Corporate Welfare
Various direct and indirect state benefits, subsidies, tax breaks, and services that meet the needs of businesses.
Corporate–Social Welfare Continuum
Analytical spectrum mapping services from pure social welfare (e.g. social care) through to pure corporate welfare (e.g. business grants and bailouts).
Social–Corporate Welfare
State provision directed at individuals but which also clearly benefits corporations (e.g. unemployment benefits, primary education).
Corporate–Social Welfare
State provision shaped as much by business demands as individual needs (e.g. infrastructure, professional training, tax breaks for private healthcare).
Systemic vs. Individual Corporate Needs
Distinction between interventions satisfying macro-economic needs of capitalism vs. those driven by lobbying of specific firms.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Systemic preservation over social equality: The 2008 bank bailouts were driven by the need to maintain economic efficiency and system survival — equality was irrelevant to the decision.
  • Ideological contraction of social welfare: Post-bailout austerity was driven by right-wing “ideological opposition to public provision” — efficiency ideology, not equality concerns.
  • Debt burden shifted to citizens: The costs of corporate failures were disproportionately shifted onto citizens via public spending cuts, drastically widening inequalities — corporate efficiency prioritised over social equality.
  • Left’s equality demands structurally ignored: Despite the left arguing corporate welfare diverts resources from the needy, the UK trajectory expanded corporate bailouts while slashing social services.
