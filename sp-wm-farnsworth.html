Farnsworth (2013)

Farnsworth, K. (2013) “Bringing corporate welfare in”, Journal of Social Policy, 42(1): 1–22.

What This Source Is

An academic journal article by Kevin Farnsworth examining the concept of “corporate welfare” — defined as direct and indirect state support provided to private businesses — and arguing that it must be systematically embedded into the broader study of social policy and the welfare state.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source directly expands on Topic 3’s exploration of the “social divisions of welfare” and the shifting roles of the state versus the market. The article significantly broadens Richard Titmuss’s classic divisions of welfare (social, fiscal, and occupational) by explicitly conceptualising corporate welfare. It challenges the binary debate of “state versus market” by demonstrating that the market is already heavily reliant on state provision, debunking the myth that capitalism thrives best without state interference. The source directly addresses whether public/state-based provision is superior to private/market-based provision by arguing that corporate and social welfare actually exist on a continuum and often compete for the same state resources. Furthermore, it highlights how the privatisation of finance and subsequent crises have led to massive state interventions (bailouts) that fundamentally altered the welfare mix, ultimately leading to austerity and the contraction of traditional social welfare.

Overview

Farnsworth’s paper argues that social policy analysis suffers from a serious omission by ignoring the vast amount of public resources directed at private corporations. Because official data is typically aggregated for general accounting rather than tracking business support, the true scale of corporate welfare remains obscured.

The author outlines five crucial reasons for scrutinising corporate welfare: it strengthens the defence of the traditional welfare state against critics; it dispels the myth that public provision undermines corporate competitiveness; it highlights how corporate and social welfare compete for finite state resources; it allows for better coordination between the two spheres; and it reinforces the argument that businesses should bear more of the tax burden for the state support they receive.

Farnsworth notes that corporate welfare creates unusual ideological alignments. Both the radical Right and radical Left oppose it, though for different reasons: the Right views it as a distortion of free-market efficiency that props up failing businesses, while the Left views it as a corrupt diversion of resources away from the needy to benefit corporate elites. Only the political centre tends to favour it, viewing state intervention as necessary to correct market failures and stabilise the economy through Keynesian demand management.

To operationalise this analysis, Farnsworth creates a “corporate–social welfare continuum.” This continuum ranges from pure social welfare (like social care, which primarily helps citizens) to pure corporate welfare (like direct business subsidies and bailouts, which exclusively aid companies). Intermediary categories include “social–corporate welfare” (e.g., unemployment benefits that aid citizens but also help businesses shed labour) and “corporate–social welfare” (e.g., tertiary education and infrastructure that heavily benefit business operations).

The paper uses OECD data to map these expenditures, revealing that countries with highly generous social welfare systems (like Sweden and Denmark) are also among the most generous in providing corporate welfare. Finally, the article contextualises its findings within the post-2008 economic crisis. It illustrates how massive state interventions to save failing banks constituted a colossal expansion of corporate welfare, the costs of which were subsequently shifted onto citizens via devastating cuts to social welfare.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Corporate welfare is deeply embedded in state operations, and the idea of a purely independent, free-market capitalism is a myth.
The author points to routine state services that businesses rely on, such as subsidised transport networks, state-funded research, public health (which boosts worker productivity), and the massive post-2008 bailouts of banks like Northern Rock and RBS.
High social welfare spending does not inherently harm economic competitiveness; rather, corporate and social welfare can be highly complementary.
Using OECD Social Expenditure (SOXC) and Revenue Statistics data, Farnsworth plots welfare spending on a scatterplot. The data reveals a positive correlation: nations like Denmark, Sweden, and France possess high levels of both social and corporate welfare, proving that heavy state intervention does not necessarily necessitate a trade-off.
The post-2008 economic crisis resulted in a massive wealth transfer that prioritised corporate survival at the direct expense of citizens’ social welfare.
The UK and other governments pumped hundreds of billions into banking systems and enacted quantitative easing. IMF data on required fiscal adjustments shows that the accumulated debt from these corporate bailouts forced subsequent governments to implement deep, devastating cuts to public social spending.
Traditional “social welfare” policies actually serve to satisfy the fundamental needs of private corporations.
Unemployment benefits are counter-cyclical (boosting consumption during downturns) and allow employers greater labour market flexibility. Furthermore, state education and training prevent “freeloading” (where firms poach staff trained by competitors) by socialising the costs of workforce preparation.

Key Terms & Concepts

Corporate Welfare
The “various benefits and services that directly, or indirectly, meet the needs of businesses.” This includes direct grants, public research, tax breaks, and bailouts.
Social Welfare
The various benefits and services that directly or indirectly meet the needs of individuals.
The Corporate–Social Welfare Continuum
A conceptual framework mapping state services along an axis based on whose needs they primarily satisfy.
Social–Corporate Welfare
State provision that brings clear benefits to both individuals and corporations, but is directed primarily towards individuals (e.g. state pensions, primary education, unemployment benefits).
Corporate–Social Welfare
State provision that brings direct benefits to businesses and is shaped as much by business demands as individual needs (e.g. professional training, infrastructure spending, tax breaks for private health care).
Systemic Needs vs. Individual Corporate Needs
The distinction between government interventions designed to satisfy the general macro-economic needs of capitalism, versus interventions driven by the lobbying activities of specific, individual firms.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • The recent changes in the UK welfare mix have been driven heavily by a prioritisation of systemic economic efficiency (saving the capitalist system) and political ideology, actively overriding concerns about social equality.
  • Systemic Preservation over Social Equality: During the 2008 financial crisis, the UK government engaged in massive interventions to bail out failing private banks (e.g. Northern Rock, RBS, HBOS) to improve market liquidity and stave off total economic collapse. This represents a massive expansion of the welfare mix towards corporations to maintain economic efficiency and system survival, regardless of the resulting inequality.
  • Ideological Contraction of Social Welfare: Following the massive expansion of corporate welfare, the UK Conservative-led coalition enacted a “sharp and equally massive contraction in social welfare provision.” The author notes this was done to recover bailout costs and to operationalise the government’s “ideological opposition to public provision,” suggesting these changes were driven by right-wing ideologies of market efficiency rather than a desire to promote equality.
  • Shifting the Debt Burden to Citizens: The interventions to save the corporate sector resulted in massive accumulated national debt. The IMF and the UK government subsequently prescribed reductions in public spending to plug fiscal holes, meaning the costs of corporate failures were disproportionately shifted onto citizens, drastically widening inequalities.
  • Efficiency-Based Critiques Used to Justify Cuts: Changes to the welfare mix are heavily influenced by Right-wing arguments that social welfare is inefficient because it “demoralises recipients and saps the strength of the productive economy,” and that state intervention distorts purely efficient free markets.
  • Ignoring Left-Wing Equality Demands: While the Left argues that corporate welfare causes inequality because it “diverts resources away from the needy” and functions as political corruption for elites, the recent trajectory of the UK welfare mix — expanding corporate bailouts while slashing social services — demonstrates that these equality-focused concerns have been structurally ignored by policymakers.
