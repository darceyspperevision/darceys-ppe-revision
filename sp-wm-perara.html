Perara (2025)

Perara, I. (2025) The Welfare Workforce: Why Mental Health Care Varies Across Affluent Democracies. Cambridge University Press. Chapter 1.

What This Source Is

The introductory chapter of a book exploring the comparative political economy of mental health care across affluent democracies, aiming to explain why some countries maintain and expand robust public mental health services while others systematically retrench them. The author focuses heavily on the historical period of “psychiatric deinstitutionalization” (from the mid-20th to late-20th centuries) and conducts paired case studies of the United States versus France, and Sweden versus Norway, to understand these diverging outcomes.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source is highly relevant to Topic 3 because it addresses the fundamental drivers behind state provision of welfare versus marketization and privatization.

  • State vs. Market: Topic 3 asks why the role of the state vis-à-vis the market has changed. This source argues that for marginalized populations who cannot participate in the market (e.g. people with severe mental illnesses like schizophrenia, who lack purchasing power), consumer demand does not drive welfare provision.
  • Privatization and Retrenchment: The text addresses the constant pressures to privatize or reduce the costs of public services. It introduces a novel perspective: rather than looking at voter demand to explain the welfare mix, we must look at the internal politics of the state apparatus — specifically, the “welfare workforce” (public sector employees).
  • Explaining the Welfare Mix: The source demonstrates that a robust state-based provision of welfare survives when public sector workers and public sector managers form a political alliance to protect their jobs and institutions against government cost-cutting and privatization efforts.

Overview

The text opens with a striking contrast during the COVID-19 pandemic: the demolition of the state-run Allentown State Hospital in the United States (due to cost-efficiency) versus the expansion of services and hiring at the Centre de Postcure in France. The author uses this dichotomy to introduce a central puzzle in political economy: Why do welfare states provide highly expensive social services to disenfranchised clients who have no political power or purchasing power to demand them?

Traditional comparative political economy struggles to answer this because mental health care does not neatly map onto established “types” of welfare states (like Esping-Andersen’s liberal, conservative, or social democratic models). For instance, both Sweden and Norway are expansive Nordic welfare states, yet they have uncharacteristically different levels of mental health care supply.

To solve this puzzle, the author introduces the theory of “supply-side policy feedback.” This theory argues that the preservation of the welfare state for the disenfranchised relies on the people who supply the services. Public sector jobs are well-compensated and protected in postindustrial economies. When the state attempts to cut costs or privatize, these workers unionize and mobilize to protect their livelihoods.

Crucially, the success of this workforce depends on an alliance with public sector managers. Unlike the private sector, where managers seek to maximize profit and minimize labor costs, public sector managers often share the same pay structures and job security concerns as their workers. When public managers are organized independently from private sector managers, they speak with a unified voice and use their bureaucratic leverage (e.g. halting policy commissions, refusing to administer services) to successfully lobby the state for increased funding alongside their workers. The presence of this coalition led to the expansion of services in France and Norway, while its absence led to severe retrenchment and hospital closures in the United States and Sweden.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The survival of public social services for marginalized groups depends on the political mobilization of the “welfare workforce,” not client demand.
The author compares Allentown, USA, and Billiers, France. Both hospitals faced government cuts and both regions suffered from deindustrialization. However, in France, the CFDT union organized massive strikes alongside staff and local mayors, successfully preserving jobs and expanding care. In the US, worker resistance lacked necessary leverage, leading to the hospital’s demolition.
A successful defense against welfare retrenchment requires a cross-class coalition between public sector workers and public sector managers.
Historical comparative analysis shows that in France, public managers organized independently of private clinic managers, allowing them to form a unified front with workers to demand resources. In the US, public managers allied with private sector psychiatrists, fracturing their interests and leaving public workers without the managerial leverage needed to stop deinstitutionalization.
Existing models of welfare capitalism fail to accurately explain the postindustrial social service sector.
The author provides cross-national quantitative data (a scatterplot of 16 affluent democracies) demonstrating that countries within the same welfare categorizations drastically diverge in their mental health care supply, proving that macro-level welfare typologies are insufficient without looking at meso-level workforce dynamics.

Key Terms & Concepts

The Welfare Workforce
Workers employed directly by the state in social services (e.g. nurses, teachers, caregivers, facility staff, and managers) who have a vested economic interest in maintaining and expanding the public sector.
Supply-Side Policy Feedback
A theoretical model arguing that the existence of a public policy creates a workforce to supply it; this workforce then mobilizes politically to protect and expand that policy, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of institutional survival.
Deinstitutionalization
An international policy trend starting post-WWII that sought to depopulate large mental asylums and ideally replace them with non-hospital, community-based care.
Public Managers
Government-employed supervisors whose jobs do not depend on political appointments. They control hiring and production but do not own the means of production, making them reliant on the state for their own wages and potentially sympathetic to rank-and-file workers.
Affluent Democracies
Advanced capitalist democracies (mainly in Western Europe, North America, and Oceania) that have transitioned to postindustrial, service-based economies.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Important Note: This specific source does not explicitly or comprehensively answer this question regarding recent UK policy. The text focuses on the United States, France, Sweden, and Norway. However, the source does discuss historical changes in England and general trends in “liberal” welfare states, which provide the following insights into efficiency versus equality.
  • Cost-Containment over Equality: The text argues that pressures to reduce costs and increase efficiency are ubiquitous forces that threaten all public services across affluent democracies. State attempts to privatize, marketize, or retrench public services are explicitly driven by the need to control expensive labor costs, rather than a desire to increase equality.
  • The English Historical Precedent: The text cites Andrew Scull’s study of psychiatric deinstitutionalization in England, noting that what ultimately closed the asylums was not a cultural shift toward equality, but the advent of the 1970s economic crisis. The state was forced to make “choices about which programs to keep and which to cut,” prioritizing efficiency over the welfare of marginalized patients.
  • The Nature of Liberal Welfare States: The UK (England) is categorized as a “liberal” welfare state alongside the United States. The text implies that in these liberal systems, the state is generally less likely to intervene in the market, making them inherently prone to retrenching social benefits for non-working, disenfranchised populations (who are deemed “undeserving”).
  • Consequences for Equality: Because changes in the welfare mix (specifically the reduction of public funding) are driven by cost-efficiency, the resulting provision of care becomes highly unequal. The author notes that where public services shrink, care relies on the private market, meaning only the “sufficiently moneyed” can afford out-of-pocket costs, leaving the destitute without support.
