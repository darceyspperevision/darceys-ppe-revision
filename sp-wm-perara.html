Perara (2025)

Perara, I. (2025) The Welfare Workforce: Why Mental Health Care Varies Across Affluent Democracies. Cambridge University Press. Chapter 1.

Overview

An introductory chapter exploring why some countries maintain robust public mental health services while others systematically retrench them. The puzzle: mental health care does not map neatly onto standard welfare typologies (e.g. Esping-Andersen) — Sweden and Norway are both Nordic welfare states but diverge dramatically in mental health supply. The core theory: supply-side policy feedback. Public sector jobs are well-paid and protected; when the state tries to cut, these workers unionise and mobilise to protect their livelihoods. Crucially, success depends on an alliance between public sector workers and public sector managers (organised independently of private-sector managers). Opens with a striking contrast: demolition of Allentown State Hospital (USA, cost-efficiency) vs. expansion at Centre de Postcure (France).

Key Arguments & Evidence

Survival of public social services for marginalised groups depends on the welfare workforce, not client demand.
Allentown (US) vs. Billiers (France): both hospitals faced cuts and deindustrialisation. French CFDT union organised strikes with local mayors and expanded care; US workers lacked leverage and the hospital was demolished.
Successful defence against retrenchment requires a cross-class coalition between public sector workers and public managers.
France: public managers organised independently of private managers, forming a unified front with workers. US: public managers allied with private psychiatrists, fracturing interests and leaving workers unable to stop deinstitutionalisation.
Existing welfare capitalism models fail to explain the postindustrial social service sector.
Cross-national quantitative data (scatterplot of 16 affluent democracies) demonstrates countries within the same welfare categories drastically diverge in mental health care supply.

Key Concepts & Definitions

The Welfare Workforce
Workers employed directly by the state in social services (nurses, teachers, caregivers, managers) with a vested economic interest in maintaining and expanding the public sector.
Supply-Side Policy Feedback
A policy creates a workforce to supply it; that workforce then mobilises politically to protect and expand the policy, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of institutional survival.
Deinstitutionalisation
Post-WWII trend depopulating psychiatric asylums and replacing them (ideally) with community-based care.
Public Managers
Government-employed supervisors whose jobs do not depend on political appointments; they control hiring and production but are reliant on the state for their wages.
Public Labor-Management Coalition
Alliance between public sector workers and managers that, when organised independently of the private sector, provides the political leverage to resist retrenchment.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Cost-containment over equality is the ubiquitous pressure: State attempts to privatise, marketize, or retrench public services are explicitly driven by labour cost control, not equality concerns.
  • English historical precedent: What closed UK asylums was not a cultural shift toward equality but the 1970s economic crisis, which forced “choices about which programs to keep and which to cut” — efficiency over welfare of marginalised patients.
  • Liberal welfare states (including UK) inherently prone to retrench: Less likely to intervene in the market, making them prone to reducing benefits for non-working disenfranchised populations deemed “undeserving.”
  • Consequences for equality: Where public services shrink, care relies on the private market; only the “sufficiently moneyed” can afford out-of-pocket costs, leaving the destitute without support.
