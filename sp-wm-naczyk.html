Naczyk (2018)

Naczyk, M. (2018) “Occupational welfare in the United Kingdom: from skill retention tool to social protection only for legitimate social risks”, in D. Natali, E. Pavolini and B. Vanhercke (eds.) Occupational welfare in Europe. Brussels: ETUI, pp. 79–102.

What This Source Is

A book chapter examining the historical development, institutional framework, and current state of Occupational Welfare (OW) in the UK. It specifically investigates how employer-provided welfare varies across two traditional social risks: loss of income due to old age (workplace pensions) and unemployment (contractual redundancy pay and short-time working arrangements).

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source fits perfectly into Topic 3 because it directly analyses one of the three key social divisions of welfare: Occupational Welfare. The paper demonstrates how the UK’s liberal welfare state model — which provides meager statutory (public) benefits — relies heavily on private, employer-provided social protection. Furthermore, the source explicitly addresses the intersection of occupational and fiscal welfare, showing how the state uses tax incentives to shape private market provision. Finally, the text explores the welfare outcomes of this mixed economy, providing data on how the privatization of social protection leads to stark inequalities based on a person’s occupation, sector, and firm size.

Overview

The paper argues that despite the minimal nature of UK statutory benefits, the development of occupational welfare is surprisingly uneven across different social risks. The UK is framed as an archetypal liberal welfare state where individuals are largely expected to provide for themselves via the market. Historically, voluntary private expenditure on social protection has been high, covering around 5.3% of the British GDP in 2011. OW originated in the 19th century via friendly societies and later expanded as unilateral management decisions by paternalistic employers to retain skilled staff and pacify labour relations.

  • Old Age (Pensions): Occupational pensions represent the most developed form of OW in the UK. Emerging from 19th-century civil service and private trust-based models, they were heavily incentivized by the state through tax exemptions. However, the landscape changed dramatically in the 1980s when the Thatcher government introduced “personal pensions,” individualising risk and undermining collective employer provision. Following major corporate scandals in the 1990s (such as the Robert Maxwell affair, where a pension fund was plundered), the state was forced to heavily increase regulation to protect workers. Today, pension access is highly stratified. Public sector workers still mostly enjoy “defined-benefit” schemes, while the private sector has shifted almost entirely to riskier “defined-contribution” schemes. Furthermore, recent governments introduced “auto-enrolment” to combat falling coverage, effectively making workplace pensions quasi-mandatory.
  • Unemployment Provision: Unlike pensions, occupational protection against unemployment — primarily contractual (enhanced) redundancy pay and short-time working arrangements — has stagnated. While the state historically encouraged these arrangements through tax exemptions to facilitate industrial restructuring, data on these benefits is incredibly scarce today. Access is highly concentrated in the public sector, large firms, and high-skill industries like finance. Trade unions have attempted to campaign for better redundancy pay and short-time working subsidies during economic crises, but employer groups like the CBI have successfully resisted these expansions.
  • The Shift in Purpose: Naczyk concludes that OW no longer primarily functions as a “skill retention tool.” Instead, the generosity and state support for OW now align with the political perception of “deservingness.” Because the elderly are viewed as deserving, both state and occupational pensions have been supported and expanded. Conversely, because political rhetoric often brands the unemployed as “skivers” or “scroungers,” occupational and statutory unemployment protections have been allowed to stagnate.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The development of occupational welfare in the UK varies dramatically depending on the social risk it covers.
The author contrasts the highly regulated, expanding field of occupational pensions (driven by state-mandated auto-enrolment) with the stagnant, largely undocumented field of contractual redundancy pay.
Reliance on occupational welfare creates deep inequalities in social protection, heavily favouring the highly skilled and those in specific sectors.
Office for National Statistics (ONS) data shows that workplace pension coverage is highest among professionals, managers, and public sector workers, while remaining very low for elementary occupations (39.4% in 2014) and workers in small firms. Similarly, a CBI survey shows average redundancy payments in finance were £21,300 compared to just £5,700 in construction.
The driving logic of occupational welfare has shifted from labour retention to political perceptions of “deservingness.”
The author points to the historical shift where Thatcher’s individualisation policies broke the traditional “retention” function of pensions. In the modern era, the author uses the political rhetoric of Conservative politicians referring to the unemployed as “skivers” to explain why unemployment OW has not been expanded, while the politically sympathetic elderly receive legislative support.

Key Terms & Concepts

Occupational Welfare (OW)
Social provision and benefits (such as pensions, redundancy pay, or medical insurance) provided by employers rather than the state, historically used to retain skilled staff.
Trust-based schemes
A legal arrangement used in pensions where assets are held in a trust fund for the sole benefit of a scheme’s members and their dependents, ensuring they are separated from the employer’s immediate corporate finances.
Auto-enrolment
A policy introduced via the Pensions Act 2008 that places a duty on employers to automatically enrol their workers into workplace pension schemes, funded by mandatory minimum contributions from the employee, employer, and the state.
Contractual (Enhanced) Redundancy Pay
Lump-sum payments offered by an employer in addition to statutory (state-mandated) redundancy pay when a worker is dismissed due to economic circumstances.
Short-time working arrangements
An employment practice where workers temporarily work reduced hours during economic downturns, sometimes with wage subsidies or compensation for lost earnings, to prevent total job loss (redundancy).
Deservingness
A political and social concept determining which vulnerable groups (e.g. the elderly vs. the unemployed) are perceived by policymakers and the public as morally worthy of welfare support.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Based strictly on the provided text, the source suggests that changes in the UK welfare mix have historically been driven by market efficiency and employer needs, but recent changes show a complex tension between managing costs (efficiency) and addressing extreme poverty (equality), heavily filtered through the lens of “deservingness.”
  • Efficiency and Cost-Cutting — Privatisation of Risk: The Thatcher government’s creation of “personal pensions” in 1988 was a drive for market efficiency that actively dismantled collective equality. It allowed workers to opt out of employer schemes, destroying the collective risk-sharing that protected vulnerable workers.
  • Efficiency and Cost-Cutting — Shift to Defined-Contribution: For the sake of corporate financial efficiency and limiting liabilities, private sector employers have overwhelmingly closed generous “defined-benefit” schemes and shifted workers onto riskier “defined-contribution” schemes, reducing equality in retirement outcomes.
  • Efficiency and Cost-Cutting — Resistance to Unemployment Protection: During economic crises, employer associations like the CBI actively resisted union demands to expand redundancy pay or implement universal short-time wage subsidies, citing that such measures would impose “significant additional costs on employers” and damage business efficiency.
  • Equality driving the mix — Auto-Enrolment: The recent introduction of auto-enrolment (Pensions Act 2008) was explicitly driven by policymakers realising that relying purely on the private market was resulting in “excessive poverty.” By forcing employers to contribute, the state is attempting to artificially generate a baseline of equality in pension access.
  • Equality driving the mix — State Regulation: In response to severe market failures and corporate abuse (e.g. the Maxwell pension plundering scandal and mis-selling scandals), the state was forced to intervene with the Pensions Acts of 1995 and 2004. These changes prioritised the protection and equality of workers over the freedom and efficiency of corporate executives.
  • The Overriding Factor — “Deservingness”: Ultimately, the author argues that neither pure efficiency nor pure equality dictates the current welfare mix. Instead, ideological perceptions of “deservingness” are the deciding factor. The state has intervened to improve the welfare mix for the elderly because they are viewed as legitimate and deserving of equality. Conversely, the state has allowed occupational protection for the unemployed to stagnate, despite clear inequalities, because political rhetoric dismisses the unemployed as inefficient “skivers” and “scroungers.”
