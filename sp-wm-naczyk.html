Naczyk (2018)

Naczyk, M. (2018) “Occupational welfare in the United Kingdom: from skill retention tool to social protection only for legitimate social risks”, in D. Natali, E. Pavolini and B. Vanhercke (eds.) Occupational welfare in Europe. Brussels: ETUI, pp. 79–102.

Overview

Examines the development of occupational welfare (OW) — employer-provided welfare — in the UK across two social risks: old age (pensions) and unemployment. The UK is framed as a liberal welfare state where individuals are expected to self-provide, with private expenditure on social protection at c.5.3% of GDP. OW originated as a paternalistic “skill retention tool”; under Thatcher it was individualised via personal pensions, and after corporate scandals (Maxwell) the state heavily increased regulation. Today, pension access is highly stratified (public sector: defined-benefit; private sector: riskier defined-contribution; auto-enrolment to combat declining coverage). Unemployment protection (contractual redundancy pay, short-time working) has stagnated. Core thesis: OW’s logic has shifted from skill retention to deservingness — generosity now tracks perceived moral worthiness.

Key Arguments & Evidence

OW development in the UK varies dramatically depending on the social risk it covers.
Occupational pensions: heavily regulated, expanding via auto-enrolment. Contractual redundancy pay: stagnant, largely undocumented, resisted by employer groups like the CBI.
Reliance on occupational welfare creates deep inequalities.
ONS data: workplace pension coverage highest among professionals and public sector; elementary occupations just 39.4% (2014). Average redundancy in finance: £21,300 vs. £5,700 in construction.
The driving logic of OW has shifted from retention to political deservingness.
Thatcher’s personal pension reforms broke the retention function. Conservative rhetoric branding the unemployed as “skivers” explains why unemployment OW stagnates while the politically sympathetic elderly receive legislative support.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Occupational Welfare (OW)
Social provision by employers beyond statutory benefits (pensions, redundancy pay, medical insurance), historically used to retain skilled staff.
Trust-Based Pension Schemes
Assets held in a trust fund for members’ sole benefit, separated from employer corporate finances.
Auto-Enrolment
Pensions Act 2008: employers must automatically enrol workers, with mandatory minimum contributions from employee, employer, and state.
Defined-Benefit vs. Defined-Contribution
DB: guaranteed pension linked to salary (mainly public sector). DC: pension depends on investment performance, shifting risk to the worker (mainly private sector).
Contractual Redundancy Pay
Enhanced lump-sum payments by employer above statutory minimums when workers are dismissed for economic reasons.
Deservingness
Political/social concept determining which groups are perceived as morally worthy of welfare support — currently favours the elderly over the unemployed.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency and cost-cutting driving the mix: Thatcher’s personal pensions (1988) dismantled collective risk-sharing in favour of market efficiency. Private sector shift to defined-contribution reduces employer liabilities.
  • Resistance to unemployment protection = efficiency over equality: CBI resisted expanding redundancy pay citing “significant additional costs on employers” — prioritising business efficiency over worker equality.
  • Auto-enrolment as an equality measure: Introduced because relying on the private market was producing “excessive poverty” in old age — a rare instance of equality concerns forcing state intervention.
  • The overriding factor is “deservingness”: Neither pure efficiency nor pure equality dictates the mix. The elderly are deemed deserving so receive support; the unemployed are stigmatised, so their protections stagnate despite clear inequality.
