Horn & Kohl (2024)

Horn, A. and Kohl, S. (2024) “Beyond trade-offs: Exploring the changing interplay of public and private welfare provision in old age and health in the historical long-run”, Journal of European Social Policy.

What This Source Is

A peer-reviewed academic article utilising a newly compiled, long-run historical dataset covering 20 OECD countries from the late 19th century to the present day. It quantitatively analyzes the relationship between public and private spending in two of the most expensive welfare domains: old-age pensions (including life insurance) and health care.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

  • State vis-à-vis the market: Topic 3 asks whether the privatization of welfare has different effects and how these roles change. This source directly answers this by challenging the traditional assumption that public and private welfare operate in a strict “zero-sum” game or trade-off.
  • Why the mix changes: This source argues that shifting “market ideologies” of governing political parties — rather than just demographic or economic pressures — are a primary driver of how the public-private mix evolves.
  • Public vs. private superiority: While it does not make a normative judgment on which is superior, it shows that historically, public and private forms of provision have often complemented each other and grown in parallel, rather than destroying one another.

Overview

The article seeks to address a major gap in comparative welfare state research: while public security systems are heavily researched, private welfare institutions (like life insurance and private health providers) are often under-researched or viewed simply as evidence of “state retreat.”

Historically, political scientists and economists have often viewed the public-private relationship through the lens of a “trade-off” or zero-sum game. In this view, either private welfare pushes out public welfare (welfare retrenchment), or public welfare crowds out private markets. However, by tracking premium and benefit payments in life insurance and health alongside public expenditure in 20 countries, the authors find that this trade-off is not a universal rule.

The authors demonstrate that longitudinally (over time), public and private provisions have generally grown in parallel. Cross-sectionally (comparing countries to one another at a single point in time), a clear negative trade-off did emerge in the post-World War II period, but this trade-off began to fade significantly after the 1980s and 2000s.

To explain why the trade-off faded, the authors test an ideological mechanism. They argue that the relationship between public and private welfare depends heavily on the “market ideology” of the governing parties. In the 1980s, neoliberal, anti-interventionist views peaked. However, since then, both center-right and center-left parties have moderated. Left parties adopted “Third Way” pragmatism that embraces market incentives, while right parties softened their calls for a minimal state. Through multivariate analysis, the authors prove that a negative public-private trade-off in healthcare only occurs when governments hold highly pronounced, anti-interventionist market ideologies. In contrast, old-age pension policies show no strict ideological trade-off, likely because pension reforms have longer time horizons and lower political visibility.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The public-private welfare relationship is not simply a zero-sum game; parallel growth is the historical norm.
A unique long-run panel dataset combining life insurance premiums/benefits and public pension/health expenditures across 20 countries. The data shows that both private and public sectors experienced joint, parallel growth for most of the 20th century.
The cross-sectional trade-off between public and private welfare peaked post-WWII but has faded in recent decades.
Scatterplots and cross-sectional correlations mapped by decade show that the negative relationship between public and private spending became insignificant in the 2000s for both health and old-age provision.
Government “market ideology” dictates whether a public-private trade-off exists in health care.
Using fixed-effects and between-country regression models tied to data from the Comparative Manifesto Project, the authors measured cabinet-level market ideology. The statistical slopes show that private health insurance only negatively impacts public health spending (a trade-off) when governments hold strong anti-interventionist ideologies. Under low market ideology, the relationship is complementary.
Old-age pensions operate on a different political logic than health care, shielding them from immediate ideological trade-offs.
Regression models for pension provision yielded horizontal slopes (no significant trade-off) regardless of whether the government had high or low market ideology. The authors support this by citing literature noting that pension policies have much longer implementation horizons and lower visibility compared to health reforms.

Key Terms & Concepts

Public–Private Trade-off / Zero-Sum Game
The theoretical assumption that public and private welfare compete for a fixed demand, meaning growth in one sector inherently comes at the expense of the other.
Crowding-Out
An economic concept describing a specific type of trade-off where the expansion of public, state-provided welfare reduces the demand for private welfare provision.
Retrenchment
The reduction or dismantling of public welfare state provisions, which is often theorized to be pushed by the rise of privatized welfare alternatives.
Market Ideology
A political stance characterized by anti-interventionist, laissez-faire (neoclassical economic), entrepreneur-friendly, and welfare-state-skeptical views.
The “Third Way”
A political movement primarily among center-left social democratic parties (emerging in the 1990s) that shifted away from traditional government intervention toward embracing market allocation, market incentives, and a “rhetoric of responsibility.”
Layering / Segmentation
A theory explaining the lack of a trade-off, which posits that public and private services cater to entirely different social layers or classes (e.g. public minimums for workers, private insurance for the upper-middle class), allowing them to co-exist independently.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Note: The provided source is a broad, quantitative study of 20 OECD countries and does not contain a specific, qualitative case study answering this exact essay question regarding the UK alone. However, based on the data and theoretical frameworks applied to the UK within the text, the following represents the source’s findings on these themes.
  • Ideology Overrides Objective Concerns: The authors argue that changes in the welfare mix are driven predominantly by shifting party ideologies regarding state vs. market, rather than purely objective economic concerns about efficiency or social concerns about equality.
  • The UK and Peak “Efficiency” Ideology (Thatcherism): The source specifically highlights the UK under Thatcherism as a prime historical example of peak “market ideology” in the 1980s. This ideology explicitly favored market allocation (efficiency) over state intervention (which traditionally champions equality), actively driving a trade-off that pushed back against public provision.
  • The Shift in the Left (The Third Way): The authors note that post-1980s, UK-style center-left parties adopted the “Third Way.” This transition meant abandoning traditional “tax-and-spend” equality politics (“politics against markets”) in favor of pragmatism that embraces market incentives. Therefore, even left-leaning changes to the mix recently feature elements of market efficiency.
  • Performance and Cost Control: The text notes that political parties are heavily influenced by “problem pressure” such as demographic aging and low economic growth. In health care, parties often embrace market incentives (like privatization or co-payments) specifically because they appeal to governments looking to improve performance and achieve cost control (budgetary relief) — which directly aligns with efficiency concerns rather than equality.
  • Convergence on Private Pillars: Across all studied countries (including the UK), governments of all political orientations have had to embrace the expansion of private pension pillars due to the exploding costs of old age. This suggests that recent changes toward a private welfare mix are highly driven by the fiscal efficiency needed to manage aging populations, a pressure no party can ignore.
