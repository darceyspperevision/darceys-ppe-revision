Horn & Kohl (2024)

Horn, A. and Kohl, S. (2024) “Beyond trade-offs: Exploring the changing interplay of public and private welfare provision in old age and health in the historical long-run”, Journal of European Social Policy.

Overview

A quantitative long-run study using a novel dataset of 20 OECD countries from the late 19th century to the present, analysing the relationship between public and private spending in pensions and health. The key challenge to conventional thinking: the public-private relationship is not a simple zero-sum trade-off. Longitudinally, both sectors have generally grown in parallel. Cross-sectionally, a negative trade-off emerged post-WWII but faded significantly after the 1980s and 2000s. The mechanism: changes in the welfare mix are driven by government market ideology (measured via Comparative Manifesto Project data). A strict trade-off in healthcare only occurs under strongly anti-interventionist governments. Old-age pensions show no strict ideological trade-off due to longer implementation horizons and lower political visibility.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The public-private welfare relationship is not zero-sum; parallel growth is the historical norm.
Long-run panel data across 20 countries shows both private and public sectors experienced joint growth for most of the 20th century.
The cross-sectional trade-off peaked post-WWII but has faded in recent decades.
Scatterplots and cross-sectional correlations show the negative relationship became statistically insignificant in the 2000s for both health and old-age provision.
Government “market ideology” dictates whether a trade-off exists in health care.
Fixed-effects regression using Comparative Manifesto Project data: private health insurance only negatively impacts public health spending when governments hold strong anti-interventionist ideologies.
Old-age pensions operate on a different political logic than health care.
Regression models for pension provision yielded horizontal slopes (no significant trade-off) regardless of government ideology, due to longer implementation horizons and lower visibility.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Public–Private Trade-off / Zero-Sum Game
Theoretical assumption that growth in public welfare inherently comes at the expense of private, or vice versa.
Crowding-Out
Expansion of public welfare reducing demand for private provision.
Retrenchment
Reduction/dismantling of public welfare provisions, often theorised to accompany rising private alternatives.
Market Ideology
Anti-interventionist, laissez-faire, welfare-state-sceptical political stance.
The Third Way
Centre-left adaptation (1990s onwards) embracing market incentives and “rhetoric of responsibility” over traditional state expansion.
Layering / Segmentation
Public and private services cater to entirely different social layers (e.g. public minimums for workers, private insurance for upper-middle class), allowing co-existence.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Ideology overrides objective concerns: Changes in the welfare mix are driven primarily by shifting party ideologies about state vs. market, not purely economic efficiency or equality concerns.
  • UK under Thatcherism = peak market ideology: The UK is cited as a prime historical example of anti-interventionist ideology actively driving a trade-off against public provision — explicitly favouring market efficiency over state-led equality.
  • Third Way = efficiency even on the left: Post-1980s UK centre-left adopted the Third Way, abandoning equality-first “tax-and-spend” politics in favour of market incentives. Even left-leaning changes now feature efficiency elements.
  • Ageing populations force private provision regardless of politics: Governments of all orientations have had to embrace private pension pillars due to exploding costs — suggesting recent changes are driven by fiscal efficiency that no party can ignore.
