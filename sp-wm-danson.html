Danson & Whittam (2011)

Danson, M., & Whittam, G. (2011) “Scotland’s Civic Society v. England’s Big Society? Diverging Roles of the VCS in Public Service Delivery”, Social Policy and Society, 10(3): 353–363.

What This Source Is

An academic journal article exploring how the devolution of powers to Scotland has led to different approaches to public service delivery compared to England. Specifically, it analyzes the differing roles expected of the Voluntary and Community Sector (VCS) under the UK government’s “Big Society” agenda versus the more collectivist policy environment in Scotland.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This source directly addresses the core themes of Topic 3 by examining the shifting boundaries between the state, the market, and the voluntary sector.

  • The role of the state vis-à-vis the voluntary sector: The text focuses heavily on the UK government’s attempt to transfer the delivery of public services from the state to the Voluntary and Community Sector (VCS). It describes this shift as a “hollowing out” of government provision, where the state acts merely as an “enabler” rather than a direct provider.
  • Privatization and Marketization: The article critiques how the VCS is being pushed to adopt market-based, competitive approaches to win public service contracts. It questions the effectiveness of this form of privatization.
  • Welfare Outcomes and Inequalities: Addressing the question of whether state-based provision is superior, the authors argue that shifting welfare delivery to the voluntary sector actually exacerbates inequalities. They highlight that volunteering has a strong class bias and that competitive bidding for social services creates local monopolies that destroy small community organizations.

Overview

The article provides a critical analysis of the UK government’s post-2010 agenda — specifically David Cameron’s “Big Society” — which sought to reduce state expenditure by utilizing the Voluntary and Community Sector (VCS) to deliver public services. The authors argue that while the terms voluntary sector, third sector, and social enterprises are often used interchangeably in policy discourse, the overarching strategy forces these organizations to act like private market competitors.

The authors trace the theoretical roots of this policy to neo-classical economic concepts, particularly Michael Porter’s theories on bringing market competition into inner-city regeneration, and the idea of utilizing social capital to solve market failures. The UK government’s approach encourages VCS organizations to bid against each other for state contracts to deliver services previously provided by the state.

However, the authors strongly critique this agenda. They argue that applying competitive market forces to social problems naturally produces “winners” and “losers,” doing nothing to resolve the uneven economic systems that caused deprivation in the first place. Furthermore, they highlight that relying on volunteerism inherently disadvantages poorer communities because volunteering is heavily skewed toward higher socio-economic groups. The contracting process itself is also flawed; large national charities often win contracts due to economies of scale, establishing monopolies that drive smaller, local grassroots organizations out of business and destroy local social capital.

In contrast to England, the authors highlight that Scotland is taking a divergent path. Despite some shared policy language, Scotland has largely rejected the “Big Society” model in favor of retaining public sector dominance in service provision. Driven by a more consensual, collectivist, and egalitarian political culture, the Scottish government utilizes the VCS in a complementary role rather than outsourcing or privatizing essential public services to disguise budget cuts.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The “Big Society” agenda relies on flawed market-based solutions and neo-classical theories to replace state welfare.
The authors point to official policy documents and David Cameron’s speeches, which aim to franchise social programs to “social entrepreneurs” and open public services up to competitive bidding. They link this to Michael Porter’s economic theories, which wrongly assume that forcing deprived areas into competitive markets will cure social exclusion.
Relying on the voluntary sector to replace the state actively increases social inequality.
The authors cite household survey data showing that volunteering has a strong class bias. People in higher socio-economic groups (A and B) volunteer at more than double the rate of those in lower socio-economic groups (D and E). Thus, relying on volunteering reproduces existing social divisions.
Competitive bidding for public services destroys local social capital rather than building it.
The authors demonstrate that public service outsourcing creates a “contestable market” where large national VCS organizations win contracts because they can offer cheaper rates through economies of scale. Once they win, they operate as monopolies, which forces smaller, local VCS groups out of business, thereby destroying the localized community networks (social capital) they provided.
Scotland is successfully diverging from the UK’s marketized welfare model.
Drawing on political scientists like Michael Keating, the authors note Scotland’s historical preference for “collectivist and egalitarian solutions.” Under both the Labour-Liberal Democrat and SNP administrations, Scotland has avoided internal markets and wholesale outsourcing, relying instead on local government as the primary service provider with the VCS offering support.

Key Terms & Concepts

Voluntary and Community Sector (VCS)
An umbrella term utilized in the paper to collectively encompass voluntary organizations, the third sector, community groups, and social enterprises.
The Big Society
A political agenda introduced by Prime Minister David Cameron aimed at shifting responsibility for community services and regeneration away from the state and onto the voluntary sector and individual communities.
Hollowing Out
The process over the last two decades wherein the state has reduced its role as a direct producer of public goods, becoming instead an “enabler” that contracts out services.
Social Capital
The networks, shared values, and community engagements that are heavily promoted by the government as a tool for regenerating deprived and disadvantaged communities.
Market Failure & Market Creation
Two economic rationales for VCS intervention. The VCS steps in where private businesses cannot operate profitably (market failure), or they create a market-based approach to solve a community need, sometimes cross-subsidizing social goals with profits (market creation).
Contestable Market
A market dynamic created by state outsourcing where organizations do not compete in a free market, but rather compete for a monopoly contract from the state. The winner gains an overwhelming competitive advantage.
Reserved and Devolved Powers
“Devolved powers” (like health, education, and local government) are controlled by the Scottish Parliament, allowing for policy divergence. “Reserved powers” (like social security, employment laws, and macroeconomic levers) remain under the control of the Westminster Parliament.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Based on the provided source, yes, recent changes in the UK welfare mix have been driven primarily by concerns about financial efficiency and state contraction, at the direct expense of equality.
  • Driven by Financial Crisis and Budget Cuts: The shift towards utilizing the VCS for public service delivery was explicitly framed by the UK government as a necessity due to the “financial crisis in public expenditure” and the impending “massive public expenditure cuts.” Privatization is being used to disguise massive resource cuts for the poorest citizens.
  • Focus on Cost Competitiveness: When services are outsourced, local authority contracts are awarded based on cost delivery. Large, national organizations achieve economies of scale and win these contracts over small, local groups purely because they offer a “more competitive rate” (i.e. higher efficiency/lower cost to the state).
  • Ignorance of the Uneven Market System: The UK government’s attempt to apply competitive market principles to welfare delivery ignores the fact that market systems naturally create “winners” and “losers.” Expanding this competitive system does nothing to reduce uneven economic development or promote equality.
  • Reproduction of Class Inequality: By transferring welfare responsibility to community volunteers, the state is ignoring equality. Volunteering is heavily skewed toward the affluent; relying on it means that socially excluded groups, the unemployed, and residents of poorer neighbourhoods are further disadvantaged and deprived of services.
  • Scotland as a Counter-Example: Scotland’s divergence highlights England’s lack of concern for equality. Because Scotland’s political culture favors “collectivist and egalitarian solutions,” it has actively resisted the UK’s push for “wholesale outsourcing and privatisation,” demonstrating that the UK’s changes are not rooted in a desire for social equality.
