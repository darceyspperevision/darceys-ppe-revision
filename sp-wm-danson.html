Danson & Whittam (2011)

Danson, M., & Whittam, G. (2011) “Scotland’s Civic Society v. England’s Big Society? Diverging Roles of the VCS in Public Service Delivery”, Social Policy and Society, 10(3): 353–363.

Overview

A critical analysis of the UK government’s post-2010 “Big Society” agenda, which sought to transfer public service delivery from the state to the Voluntary and Community Sector (VCS). The authors trace roots to neo-classical economic theories (Michael Porter) and argue the VCS is being forced to adopt market-based, competitive approaches. Key critique: competitive bidding allows large national charities to win contracts via economies of scale, establishing monopolies that destroy smaller local organisations and the social capital they generated. Scotland is presented as a successful counter-example — retaining public sector dominance and using the VCS in a complementary role rather than as a privatisation vehicle.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The “Big Society” agenda relies on flawed market-based theory to replace state welfare.
The authors link government policy to Michael Porter’s economic theories, which wrongly assume competitive markets can cure social exclusion in deprived areas.
Relying on the voluntary sector actively increases social inequality.
Household survey data: people in socioeconomic groups A and B volunteer at more than double the rate of groups D and E. Relying on volunteering reproduces existing class divisions.
Competitive bidding for public services destroys local social capital.
Large national VCS organisations win on cost (economies of scale), operate as monopolies, and drive smaller local groups out of business — destroying the community networks they provided.
Scotland is successfully diverging from the UK’s marketised welfare model.
Scotland’s collectivist political culture led both Labour-LibDem and SNP administrations to avoid internal markets and wholesale outsourcing, using local government as the primary provider.

Key Concepts & Definitions

VCS / Third Sector
Umbrella term for voluntary organisations, charities, community groups, and social enterprises.
Big Society
Cameron’s agenda shifting responsibility for community services away from the state onto VCS and communities — critics argue it masks budget cuts.
Hollowing Out
The state reduces its role as direct producer of public goods, becoming an “enabler” that contracts out services.
Social Capital
Networks, shared values, and community engagements governments promote as tools for regenerating deprived areas.
Contestable Market
Organisations compete not in a free market but for a monopoly state contract; the winner gains overwhelming competitive advantage.
Reserved vs. Devolved Powers
Health, education, and local government are devolved to Scotland, allowing policy divergence from Westminster.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Driven by budget cuts, not equality: The UK government explicitly framed the shift to VCS as a response to “massive public expenditure cuts” — privatisation used to disguise resource reduction for the poorest.
  • Cost competitiveness over equality: Outsourcing contracts awarded on cost delivery, favouring large national organisations over local ones purely because they offer a “more competitive rate.”
  • Reproduction of class inequality: Transferring welfare to volunteers ignores equality — volunteering is skewed toward the affluent, so socially excluded groups and poorer communities are further disadvantaged.
  • Scotland as counter-example: Scotland’s preference for “collectivist and egalitarian solutions” has resisted wholesale outsourcing — demonstrating England’s changes are not rooted in equality concerns.
