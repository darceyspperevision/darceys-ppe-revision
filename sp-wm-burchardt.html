Burchardt & Obolenskaya (2016)

Burchardt, T. & Obolenskaya, P. (2016) Public & private welfare activity in England, CASEPaper 193, London: CASE, LSE.

Overview

A 2016 empirical working paper from LSE’s Centre for Analysis of Social Exclusion (CASE) that tracks the shifting boundaries between public and private welfare in England across five policy sectors over 34 years (1979/80 to 2013/14). The paper uses the “Wheels of Welfare” framework, which evaluates welfare activity across three dimensions: finance (public tax vs. private), provision (public vs. private/independent), and decision/regulation (state control vs. user choice). Key sector findings: Housing underwent outright privatisation (pure private rising from 58% to 82%); Social Care saw pure public collapse from 66% to 26%, replaced by contracting out; Health remained largely publicly financed but saw a shift to private provision; Education saw rising private tuition and the student loans shift; Income Maintenance was the most stable. Total welfare activity trebled in real terms, but private growth outpaced public.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The burden of welfare is shifting toward individually financed and provided means.
The “pure public” share fell significantly while “pure private” climbed to near parity with the public sector (40% public vs. 38% private when including housing).
Privatisation looks vastly different depending on the specific policy area.
Housing relies on pure private activity; Social Care transformed through contracting out; Education and Health remain mostly public but with creeping private elements.
The welfare state is not shrinking in absolute terms, but growth is mostly private.
Total welfare spending grew from 26.1% to 39.6% of GDP — a massive expansion — but the public share of that growth shrank significantly.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Wheels of Welfare
A conceptual framework that unbundles welfare activity by assessing who finances, who provides, and who controls it — moving beyond the simple state/market binary.
Pure Public
Tax-financed, publicly provided, with little to no user choice (e.g. NHS emergency treatment).
Pure Private
Individually initiated and market-purchased welfare (e.g. private medical insurance, private tutoring).
Contracting Out
State commissions and pays for services, but delivery is by independent/private organisations (e.g. local authorities paying private care homes).
Decision Dimension
Assesses degree of user agency: can they choose how much to have, who provides it, and are there viable alternatives?

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Efficiency via contraction: The Coalition’s “Big Society” agenda explicitly aimed to shrink government and let private/voluntary sectors take on welfare — a cost-saving efficiency drive, not an equality drive.
  • Contracting out as efficiency: The Social Care shift from 66% pure public to 35% contracted out, and Health’s 65% growth in NHS purchasing from non-NHS providers, reflects a belief that private markets deliver state-funded services more efficiently.
  • Pure private growth harms equality: Tuition fees shifted the financial burden from collective taxation to individuals; housing privatisation left pure public provision at just 5%, pricing out lower-income citizens — efficiency concerns overriding equality.
