Burchardt & Obolenskaya (2016)

Burchardt, T. & Obolenskaya, P. (2016) Public & private welfare activity in England, CASEPaper 193, London: CASE, LSE.

What This Source Is

A 2016 working paper documenting the shifting boundaries between public and private actors in the English welfare state over a 34-year period, analysing data from 1979/80, 1995/96, 1999/00, 2007/08, and 2013/14. It is an empirical, statistical report tracking changes across five major welfare sectors.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

This paper serves as a foundational empirical text for understanding the debates outlined in Topic 3. It directly measures how the role of the state vis-à-vis the market has changed over time by breaking down welfare activity into three distinct dimensions: who finances it, who provides it, and who controls/decides on it. By quantifying the exact degree of “privatisation of finance, provision and regulation” across five different social policy sectors, the source provides the hard data needed to assess the changing social divisions of welfare. It allows you to see that privatization is not a single process, but can involve the state paying private companies to deliver services (contracting out), or individuals privately purchasing their own welfare to replace or top-up state services.

Overview

The paper assesses total spending (public and private) on welfare in England using the Wheels of Welfare framework — evaluating welfare services across three dimensions: Finance (public tax vs. private out-of-pocket/insurance), Provision (publicly owned organisations vs. independent/private providers), and Decision/Regulation (state control vs. user choice/market alternatives). Changes are tracked across five major sectors:

  • Education: Remains dominated by public finance and provision, but “pure public” share has steadily declined. “Pure private” spending grew from 5.9% in 1979/80 to 22% in 2013/14, driven by private tuition and the shift from public grants to tuition fees and student loans. Academies and Free Schools are classified as “public” following National Accounts definitions.
  • Health: Remains largely publicly financed, but “pure public” healthcare declined from 71% (1979/80) to 59% (2013/14), primarily because the state increasingly purchases healthcare from independent providers — publicly funded but privately provided healthcare jumped to 25% by 2013/14. Pure private spending (private medical insurance) remained stable at around 13% since the mid-1990s.
  • Housing: The most extensively privatised sector. “Pure private” skyrocketed from 58% (1979/80) to 82% (2013/14), driven by Right-to-Buy, expansion of owner-occupation, rising property values, and increased private renting. Pure public provision plummeted from 18% to just 5%.
  • Income Maintenance: The most stable sector, though “public decision” (state control) over funds has fallen. Growth of private occupational pensions has increased the “private decision” element.
  • Social Care: The most dramatic structural transformation. “Pure public” provision collapsed from 66% (1979/80) to just 26% (2013/14). The gap was filled by an explosion in “contracting out” (Local Authorities paying private companies — now 35%, the largest single share) and a major increase in pure private spending (rising to 28%).

Overall: Total welfare activity trebled in real terms from £225bn (26% of GDP) in 1979/80 to £694.5bn (almost 40% of GDP) in 2013/14. However, growth was disproportionately driven by privately financed and privately provided welfare.

Key Arguments & Evidence

Argument 1: The burden of welfare is shifting toward individually financed and provided means.
The proportion of “pure public” activity fell significantly across the 34 years, while the share of “pure private” activity climbed to reach near parity with the public sector (40% public vs. 38% private when including housing).
Argument 2: The privatization of the welfare state looks vastly different depending on the specific policy area.
Housing relies heavily on “pure private” activity; Social Care was transformed primarily through “contracting out” (public finance/private provision); Education and Health remain mostly public but with creeping private elements.
Argument 3: The welfare state is not shrinking in absolute terms; total welfare activity has expanded enormously, but the growth is largely in the private sector.
Total welfare spending grew from 26.1% of GDP to 39.6% of GDP over the period studied, representing a massive absolute increase, but the share of that pie belonging to public finance/provision shrank.

Key Terms & Concepts

Wheels of Welfare
A conceptual framework used to unbundle the welfare state by assessing who finances, who provides, and who controls welfare services.
Pure Public
Welfare activity that is tax-financed, provided by a publicly-owned organisation, and features little to no user choice (e.g. emergency treatment in an NHS hospital).
Pure Private
Welfare activity undertaken by individual initiative and purchased entirely in the free market (e.g. private medical insurance, private tutoring).
Contracting Out (Public Finance / Private Provision)
Services commissioned and paid for by the state but delivered by independent, non-state organisations (e.g. Local Authorities paying independent care homes for adult social care).
Decision Dimension (Control/Regulation)
A metric capturing the degree of agency and choice a user has. Three tests: can the user choose how much to have, can they choose who provides it, and are there viable alternatives? Highly regulated activities are classified as “public decision.”

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • The pursuit of “Efficiency” through shrinking the state: Recent changes, particularly under the 2010 Coalition Government’s “Big Society” agenda, were explicitly intended to shrink “big government” and give the private and voluntary sectors a greater role in delivering public welfare. This reflects a drive for cost-saving efficiency over state-led equality.
  • Efficiency driven by “Contracting Out”: The massive shift in Social Care (from 66% pure public to 35% contracted out) and Health (where NHS purchase of non-NHS healthcare grew 65% between 2007 and 2013) demonstrates a government belief that private markets can deliver state-funded services more efficiently than state monopolies.
  • A disregard for Equality via “Pure Private” growth: The evidence strongly suggests equality has been a secondary concern. Gaps left by reductions in public spending have forced individuals to step in. “Pure private” spending on education surged as middle-class families increasingly chose to top-up state schooling with private tuition.
  • Privatization of Finance harms Equality: The paper documents a dramatic shift from public grants to private tuition fees and student loans in higher education. By shifting the financial burden directly onto the individual user rather than collective taxation, the welfare mix has inherently moved away from universal equality toward individual financial responsibility.
  • Housing inequality: The near-total privatization of housing (with pure public activity dropping to 5%) was caused by policies like Right-to-Buy and massive cuts to social housing subsidies. This indicates that generating private market efficiency and homeownership was prioritized over maintaining an equal, state-subsidized baseline of housing for lower-income citizens.
