Powell (2019)

Powell, M. (ed.) (2019) Understanding the Mixed Economy of Welfare, Bristol: Policy Press (Chapter 1 and 2).

What This Source Is

This source consists of excerpts from the first two chapters of the textbook Understanding the mixed economy of welfare (2nd edition). Chapter 1 is authored by Martin Powell, and Chapter 2 is authored by John Stewart. The text serves as an academic introduction to social policy, specifically focusing on the mechanisms through which welfare is distributed beyond the traditional boundaries of the state. It explores the roles of the market, voluntary, and informal sectors, as well as the “hidden” welfare state that operates through the tax system and employment benefits.

How This Source Fits into Topic 3

  • The State vis-à-vis Other Sectors: The text defines the “mixed economy of welfare” (MEW) as comprising four key components: the state, the market, the voluntary sector, and the informal (family) sector.
  • Historical Changes in the UK: Chapter 2 explicitly tracks how the balance between these sectors has shifted over time, moving from localized/voluntary provision in the 19th century, to the “classic welfare state” dominance of the central state post-1945, and then toward the marketization and privatization trends from the 1970s onwards.
  • Social Divisions of Welfare: The source explores Richard Titmuss’s concept of the “social division of welfare” (SDW), dedicating sections to its three pillars: statutory (public), occupational (company/fringe benefits), and fiscal (tax-based) welfare.
  • Privatization of Finance, Provision, and Regulation: A major theme of the text is moving beyond a “one-dimensional” view of welfare (who provides it) to a “three-dimensional” framework that analyzes how welfare is financed, provided, and regulated.

Overview

Chapter 1 (Martin Powell) introduces the foundational concepts of the MEW and the SDW, arguing that mainstream social policy often suffers from a narrow focus on the visible “welfare state” while neglecting the wider, often invisible, mechanisms of welfare. Powell argues that to understand social policy, one must view it as a constantly shifting frontier between public and private realms. He advocates for analyzing welfare across three distinct dimensions: provision (who owns/delivers the service), finance (who pays for the service), and regulation (who controls or inspects the service). The chapter also explores the ideological divides — specifically how the political left tends to favor state intervention to remedy “market failure,” whereas the political right champions the market, voluntary, and informal sectors to remedy “state failure” and economic inefficiency.

Chapter 2 (John Stewart) provides a historical perspective on the MEW, critiquing the traditional “Whig” history of welfare which incorrectly portrays a linear, inevitable progression toward a state-dominated system. Stewart demonstrates that the UK has always possessed a mixed economy of welfare. He traces this through different eras: the laissez-faire 19th century where local authorities and charities dominated; the early 20th-century Liberal reforms which increased central state involvement; the 1940s establishment of the “classic welfare state” (like the NHS) built on post-war consensus; and the post-1970s era where Conservative and New Labour governments increasingly emphasized market supremacy and the voluntary sector to roll back state frontiers. The chapter utilizes a fictional case study of “Margaret” (experiencing the comprehensive state welfare of the 1950s) and her daughter “Jane” (experiencing a fragmented, market-dependent welfare system in the 1980s) to practically illustrate these historical shifts.

Key Arguments & Evidence

The “welfare state” is only a fraction of total social welfare, much of which is “hidden” or invisible to the public.
In the US, 60% of people benefiting from the home mortgage interest deduction claim they “have not used a government social program.” In the UK, fiscal welfare — such as tax relief for non-state pensions — is a massive hidden benefit, officially estimated to cost the government £21 billion. Furthermore, occupational welfare (employer contributions) was equivalent to around 7% of GDP in 2000, which is significantly larger than the 3.8% from statutory provision.
A one-dimensional analysis of welfare (focusing only on who provides a service) is inadequate.
The state can intervene heavily without actually providing the service. For example, the state uses public finance to pay the fees for individuals living in private residential homes, or funds charities that help the homeless. Similarly, the state exercises vast power through regulation, such as rent controls on private landlords or inspections via bodies like OFSTED, demonstrating that control is not limited to ownership.
The different sectors of the welfare mix are not “functional equivalents” that can simply be swapped for one another.
Substituting state care with informal care inherently relies on unpaid female labor, leading to increased gender inequality (the “sexual division of welfare”). Additionally, replacing state provision with charity ignores the concept of citizen entitlement, as no individual has a legal “right” to receive charity.
The dominance of the central state in welfare provision was a historical anomaly rather than the inevitable endpoint of social policy.
Prior to 1945, non-state sectors were the dominant providers of welfare in Britain. From 1979 onwards, Conservative governments successfully challenged the post-war consensus, altering the mixture of provision by rolling back state frontiers and shifting reliance back to the family, the private sector, and the voluntary sector.

Key Terms & Concepts

Mixed Economy of Welfare (MEW) / Welfare Pluralism
The provision of welfare services through a combination of the state, the market, the voluntary sector, and the informal sector (family/friends).
Social Division of Welfare (SDW)
A concept developed by Richard Titmuss highlighting that welfare is delivered not just through social services, but through three distinct systems: statutory, fiscal, and occupational.
Statutory Welfare (Social/Public Welfare)
The visible part of the welfare state, consisting of publicly provided goods and services.
Occupational Welfare
The “fringe benefits” of employment, such as subsidized company pensions, health insurance, or company cars.
Fiscal Welfare
Benefits delivered indirectly through the taxation system, such as tax relief on mortgages, pensions, and health insurance. These are sometimes called “upside-down benefits” because they often regressively favor higher-income earners.
The “Hidden” or “Submerged” Welfare State
Social mechanisms, predominantly fiscal and occupational welfare, that are not traditionally visible or considered part of government social policy by citizens.
Three-Dimensional Framework
An analytical model that evaluates welfare programs based on three axes: Provision (who runs it), Finance (who pays for it), and Regulation (who controls/inspects it).
Commodification
The process of transforming a free public service into a “fee” (or paid) service within the private market.
Whig History of Welfare
An outdated historical paradigm that viewed the expansion of central state intervention in welfare as a story of constant, inevitable progress culminating in the post-war “classic welfare state.”

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • The recent changes in the UK welfare mix have been driven heavily by a political and ideological shift that prioritizes efficiency over equality.
  • The Drive for Market Efficiency: Since the 1970s, and particularly under Conservative administrations from 1979 to 1997, there was a deliberate attempt to “roll back the frontiers of the state” in favor of the market, voluntary, and informal sectors. Advocates for this greater role for the market explicitly pointed to “state failure,” arguing that the state is an inefficient producer of services. Consequently, reforms like contracting out, quasi-markets, and privatization were introduced under the premise that the market is more efficient.
  • The Rejection of Dependency: In tandem with efficiency concerns, the political right argued that extensive state welfare bred dependency and was morally corrosive, advocating instead for a “welfare society” driven by commercial and voluntary solutions. This indicates a shift away from universal equality and toward individual/market reliance.
  • Sacrificing Equality in the Social Divisions of Welfare: Shifting welfare out of the statutory realm and into the occupational and fiscal realms is inherently regressive. Richard Titmuss argued that fiscal and occupational welfare disproportionately benefit those with higher incomes (acting as “upside-down benefits”), meaning the expansion of the “hidden welfare state” actively undermines economic equality.
  • Worsening Gender Inequality: Shifting the burden of welfare from the state to the “informal sector” (the family) is not a neutral, efficient swap. Feminists point out the “double equation” that community care equals care by the family, which in practice equals care by women. Therefore, relying on the informal sector to increase state efficiency directly leads to increasing gender inequality.
  • Erosion of Social Citizenship: Shifting welfare to the private or voluntary sectors removes the “entitlement” aspect of welfare. The left argues that these components are not “functional equivalents” because one has no fundamental “right” to receive charity, fundamentally eroding social equality and citizenship rights in the pursuit of a smaller, more efficient state.
  • Ultimately, the text demonstrates that the post-1970s shifts in the welfare mix were explicitly underpinned by the “alleged primacy of the market” and its purported ability to efficiently solve social problems. However, as critics note, these changes prioritize economic efficiency and reduced state spending at the direct expense of gender equality, income equality, and universal citizenship rights.
