Powell (2019)

Powell, M. (ed.) (2019) Understanding the Mixed Economy of Welfare, Bristol: Policy Press (Chapter 1 and 2).

Overview

The foundational textbook for this topic. Chapter 1 (Powell) introduces the Mixed Economy of Welfare (MEW) and Social Division of Welfare (SDW), arguing mainstream social policy ignores the “hidden welfare state” of fiscal and occupational welfare. Chapter 2 (Stewart) provides a historical corrective to “Whig history” narratives of inevitable state expansion — demonstrating that the UK has always had a mixed economy. Uses fictional case studies of “Margaret” (1950s comprehensive state welfare) and “Jane” (1980s fragmented market-dependent welfare) to illustrate the shift. Key framework: welfare must be analysed across three dimensions: provision (who owns/delivers), finance (who pays), and regulation (who controls).

Key Arguments & Evidence

The welfare state is only a fraction of total social welfare — much is hidden.
In the US, 60% of home mortgage interest deduction beneficiaries claim they’ve “never used a government social program.” UK fiscal welfare (tax relief for non-state pensions) officially costs £21bn. Occupational welfare was c.7% of GDP vs. 3.8% from statutory provision.
A one-dimensional analysis (who provides) is inadequate.
The state can pay private care home fees (public finance/private provision), fund charities, or control via regulation (OFSTED) without owning the service. Control ≠ ownership.
Sectors are not functional equivalents — they cannot simply be swapped.
Substituting state care with informal care relies on unpaid female labour (the “sexual division of welfare”). Replacing state provision with charity removes legal entitlement — no one has a right to receive charity.
State dominance in welfare was a historical anomaly, not the inevitable endpoint.
Prior to 1945, non-state sectors dominated British welfare. From 1979, Conservative governments successfully challenged the post-war consensus and rolled back state frontiers.

Key Concepts & Definitions

Mixed Economy of Welfare (MEW)
Provision of welfare through the state, market, voluntary sector, and informal sector (family/friends).
Social Division of Welfare (SDW)
Titmuss’s framework: welfare delivered through statutory, fiscal, and occupational systems — with fiscal and occupational often invisible.
Fiscal Welfare
“Upside-down benefits” — tax reliefs on mortgages, pensions, health insurance that disproportionately benefit higher earners.
Commodification
Transforming a free public service into a fee-based market service.
Three-Dimensional Framework
Analyse welfare across Provision (who runs it), Finance (who pays), and Regulation (who controls).
Whig History of Welfare
Outdated view of state expansion as inevitable, linear progress — Stewart argues the mix has always been contested.

On the Essay Question

✦ Have recent changes in the welfare mix in the UK resulted more from concerns about efficiency than from concerns about equality? ✦

  • Market efficiency ideology drove post-1970s changes: The political right explicitly cited “state failure” and market efficiency to justify rolling back state frontiers, contracting out, and quasi-markets.
  • Equality sacrificed via fiscal and occupational welfare expansion: Titmuss showed fiscal and occupational welfare are regressive — the expansion of the “hidden welfare state” actively undermines economic equality.
  • Gender equality undermined: Shifting to informal care (family) increases gender inequality, as “community care = care by the family = care by women” in practice.
  • Erosion of social citizenship: Moving to voluntary/private sectors removes legal entitlement — eroding equality and citizenship rights in pursuit of a smaller, more efficient state.
